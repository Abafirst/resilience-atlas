# Cognitive-Narrative Dimension: Skills Inventory

> *"We are the stories we tell about ourselves."*
> — Adapted from cognitive narrative therapy tradition

---

## Overview

The Cognitive-Narrative dimension of the IATLAS addresses the way human beings make sense of their lives through thought and story. Resilience is not only about what happens to us — it is profoundly shaped by how we interpret, narrate, and construct meaning from our experiences. The cognitive-narrative lens captures both the micro-level of individual thought patterns and the macro-level of life narrative: the story we tell about who we are, where we come from, and where we are going.

"Cognitive" refers to the processes of thinking — recognizing patterns, evaluating situations, interpreting events, and making attributions. "Narrative" refers to the storied dimension of human experience — the way we organize events into sequences, characters, and meaning over time. Together, these constitute one of the most powerful resilience dimensions in the IATLAS framework.

Research across cognitive therapy (Beck), narrative therapy (White & Epston), ACT defusion and cognitive flexibility, and positive psychology's post-traumatic growth framework converge on a shared insight: how we think and story our experience powerfully shapes our resilience. And these cognitive-narrative processes are learnable, modifiable, and developable with appropriate support.

## Core Skill Domains

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **Reframing** | The capacity to interpret events through alternative, more adaptive lenses without denying factual reality |
| **Perspective-Taking** | The ability to see situations, events, and one's own narrative from multiple viewpoints, including those of others |
| **Growth Mindset** | The belief that abilities, strengths, and character qualities are developable through effort, not fixed by nature |
| **Adaptive Thinking** | The broader repertoire of cognitive skills that support flexible, accurate, and constructive interpretation of experience |
| **Story Revision** | The capacity to revise one's self-narrative in ways that incorporate adversity, growth, and complexity |

## Skills Inventory

### Level 1: Foundational Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Identifying Automatic Thoughts** | The ability to notice and name the spontaneous thoughts that occur in response to events | Can identify at least one automatic thought per day; distinguishes thoughts from facts; uses a thought record at foundational level |
| **Basic Reframing** | Generating at least one alternative interpretation of a challenging event | Can state at least one alternative perspective on a situation; does not immediately dismiss reframes; begins with less challenging events |
| **Distinguishing Thought from Fact** | Recognizing the difference between a thought ("This will never get better") and a factual statement | Can apply "Is this a thought or a fact?" test to common automatic thoughts; begins to distinguish opinion from observation |
| **Growth vs. Fixed Mindset Recognition** | Identifying when one's self-talk reflects a fixed mindset (abilities are fixed) vs. growth mindset (abilities are developable) | Can name a fixed mindset statement they use; can convert it to a growth mindset equivalent; begins to notice mindset orientation in daily experience |
| **Noticing Cognitive Patterns** | Recognizing recurring patterns in one's own thinking (catastrophizing, mind-reading, all-or-nothing thinking) | Can identify at least 2 personal cognitive patterns; recognizes when they are occurring in real-time |
| **Simple Story Retelling** | The ability to retell a personal story with slight alterations in emphasis or interpretation | Can retell a personal story with a different emphasis; identifies what changes when the telling changes |
| **Identifying Narrative Themes** | Recognizing recurring themes in one's personal narrative | Can identify at least 2 recurring themes in their life story (e.g., "I always fail when I try something new"); notes these without immediate self-criticism |
| **Cognitive Flexibility Basics** | Beginning to demonstrate flexibility in thinking — considering "what else might be true" | Can generate at least one alternative interpretation when prompted; shows initial willingness to examine habitual cognitive patterns |

---

### Level 2: Intermediate Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Structured Reframing** | Using a deliberate, multi-step process to examine evidence for and against a thought and generate balanced alternatives | Completes a full cognitive restructuring record independently; generates evidence on both sides; creates a balanced alternative |
| **Multiple Perspective-Taking** | Generating 3+ perspectives on a situation, including the perspectives of other people | Can articulate at least 3 perspectives; includes perspectives that challenge their own; demonstrates understanding of how position shapes interpretation |
| **Challenge Response Planning** | Preparing adaptive cognitive responses to anticipated challenging situations before they occur | Creates written or verbal challenge response plans; deploys them effectively in real situations; reports reduced reactivity in planned situations |
| **Post-Traumatic Narrative Reconstruction** | Revising one's narrative about a traumatic experience to include growth, survival, and meaning alongside suffering | Can articulate the adversity and the growth in the same narrative; does not require resolution or silver-lining framing; demonstrates narrative complexity |
| **Adaptive Attribution** | Making explanations for events that are accurate, flexible, and adaptive (rather than global, stable, internal self-blame) | Distinguishes between explanations that help and those that harm; makes situational rather than dispositional attributions for setbacks; acknowledges own contribution without self-flagellation |
| **Narrative Coherence Building** | Constructing a life narrative that is internally coherent, temporally organized, and meaning-bearing | Can tell their life story with a beginning, middle, and present; narrative includes both difficulty and progress; reports increased sense of identity clarity |
| **Strengths-Based Story** | Identifying and amplifying the strengths, competencies, and values visible in one's personal history | Can identify at least 5 personal strengths visible in their life story; uses strengths as narrative anchors; narrative is not exclusively problem-saturated |
| **Metacognitive Awareness** | Thinking about one's own thinking — observing cognitive processes from a meta-level | Can describe their own thinking process (not just content); identifies when their thinking is helpful vs. unhelpful; uses metacognitive awareness to self-regulate |

### Level 3: Advanced Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Narrative Transformation** | Fundamentally revising a life narrative to integrate adversity as a contributor to identity and values | Demonstrates narrative coherence that genuinely integrates suffering and growth; narrative transformation is embodied, not merely cognitive |
| **Witnessing and Bearing Witness** | The capacity to hold another person's story with full, non-judgmental attention; to be a witness to another's experience | Demonstrates deep witnessing in session and relationships; does not redirect, minimize, or pathologize others' stories |
| **Story as Advocacy** | Using one's personal narrative as a vehicle for social change, community healing, or collective meaning | Has shared their story in a public or community context for the benefit of others; demonstrates integration of personal and collective narrative |
| **Teaching Reframing** | Effectively conveying reframing and perspective-taking skills to others in educational or therapeutic contexts | Explains reframing clearly; adapts to learner's cognitive style; creates conditions for perspective-taking in others |
| **Collective Narrative** | Identifying and working with the narratives of communities, families, and cultural groups — including intergenerational stories | Can articulate their family or community narrative; identifies how collective stories shape individual experience; works with collective narrative as a resilience resource |
| **Wisdom Integration** | Synthesizing life experience, including suffering, into a coherent wisdom that benefits self and others | Speaks from integrated personal experience; offers perspective with humility; uses narrative wisdom to support others |
| **Cultural Narrative Analysis** | Understanding how cultural narratives shape individual experience and identity | Can identify dominant cultural narratives that have affected their self-story; deconstructs and, where appropriate, resists limiting cultural narratives |
| **Legacy Story Construction** | Constructing a forward-looking narrative about what one wants to contribute and be remembered for | Has a clear, articulate legacy narrative; makes current decisions with legacy in view; can teach legacy narrative construction to others |

## Cross-Dimensional Connections

| Connected Dimension | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Agentic-Generative** | Narrative of agency — the story that "I can act effectively" — directly supports agentic behavior; growth mindset is both cognitive and agentic |
| **Relational-Connective** | Social support is often delivered through witnessing and narrative; relational ruptures have narrative dimensions; shared narrative builds community |
| **Spiritual-Reflective** | Meaning-making is the intersection of narrative and spiritual work; the spiritual autobiography is a cognitive-narrative practice |
| **Emotional-Adaptive** | Emotions are embedded in narratives; narrative reconstruction can transform emotional relationship to the past |
| **Somatic-Regulative** | Body narrative — the story we tell about our body — shapes physical self-care; somatic memories are narrative |

## Trauma-Sensitive Cognitive Work

Cognitive and narrative work with trauma-exposed clients requires particular care:

- **Titrate intensity**: Do not begin with the most traumatic material; build narrative skills with less intense content first
- **Don't rush reconstruction**: Narrative transformation of trauma is a long, nonlinear process; respect the time it takes
- **Avoid forced meaning-making**: Do not push clients to find silver linings or lessons in trauma before they are ready
- **Attend to dissociation**: Trauma recall can trigger dissociation; monitor closely and have grounding available
- **Validate suffering**: Cognitive reframing of trauma must never minimize or invalidate the reality of what happened

## Assessment Considerations

Baseline cognitive-narrative assessment should include:

1. **Narrative access**: Can the client tell a coherent personal story?
2. **Cognitive flexibility**: Can the client generate alternative perspectives?
3. **Narrative themes**: What recurring themes organize the client's self-story?
4. **Attribution style**: How does the client explain good and bad events?
5. **Mindset orientation**: Fixed vs. growth orientation
6. **Narrative trauma indicators**: Is the life narrative disrupted, fragmentary, or dominated by traumatic content?
