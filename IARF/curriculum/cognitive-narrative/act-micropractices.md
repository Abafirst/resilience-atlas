# Cognitive-Narrative Dimension: ACT Micropractices

---

## Overview

The Cognitive-Narrative dimension is where ACT's cognitive defusion processes are most centrally relevant. While traditional cognitive therapy focuses on changing the content of thoughts, ACT's defusion approach changes the relationship to thoughts — enabling clients to observe thoughts without being controlled by them.

This document presents eight core ACT micropractices specifically designed for the Cognitive-Narrative dimension of The Resilience Atlas™ IATLAS. Together, they build the capacity for cognitive flexibility, perspective-taking, and narrative freedom that constitute advanced cognitive-narrative resilience.

---

## Cognitive Defusion: Overview and Rationale

Cognitive fusion — being "stuck" to thoughts, treating them as literally true and responding to them as if they were the world rather than mental events — is one of the primary mechanisms of psychological suffering. When we are fused with a thought like "I am fundamentally flawed," we do not have the thought; we become it. The thought becomes the lens through which we see everything, and behavior contracts around it.

Defusion involves stepping back from thoughts — seeing them as mental events (words, images, memories) rather than as facts or commands. Defusion does not require changing, suppressing, or challenging the thought. It simply changes its hold.

This shift — from being a thought to having a thought — is one of the most powerful interventions available for cognitive-narrative resilience.

## Practice 1: Leaves on a Stream

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Client is ruminating; caught in a stream of troubling thoughts

### Full Guided Script

"I'd like to try a practice that many people find useful for relating differently to thoughts. It's sometimes called Leaves on a Stream.

Close your eyes, or soften your gaze. Take a few slow breaths.

Imagine you're sitting beside a gently flowing stream. It's a peaceful setting — the stream is moving slowly, there are leaves falling from the trees along the bank, and each leaf drifts downstream on the surface of the water.

[Pause 15 seconds]

Now: as thoughts arise in your mind — any thoughts, all thoughts — place each one on a leaf and watch it float downstream.

It doesn't matter what the thought is. It could be a worry, a self-criticism, a plan, a memory, a judgment about the exercise itself. Whatever arises, place it on a leaf. Watch it drift downstream. Don't try to make it go faster or slower. Just watch.

[Allow 3–5 minutes of silent practice]

If you find yourself lost in a thought — swept up in it rather than watching it float by — that's fine. Notice that it happened, and gently return to the banks of the stream.

[Pause]

Slowly come back to the room. Take a breath. Open your eyes when you're ready.

What did you notice? Were certain thoughts harder to let float by? Were there thoughts you kept getting swept into?"

## Practice 2: Milk, Milk, Milk (Classic Defusion Exercise, Adapted)

**Duration**: 5–10 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion
**Best Used When**: Introducing defusion for the first time; demonstrating how repetition can strip words of their literal impact

### Instructions

"I'd like to start with a brief demonstration. I'm going to ask you to say the word 'milk' out loud, repeatedly, for 30 seconds. Ready?

[Client says 'milk' repeatedly for 30 seconds]

What happened to the word? Did it start to sound strange? Lose some of its milk-ness?

[Explore]

What we just demonstrated is that words are sounds — and when we repeat them long enough, we can start to see them as just sounds, rather than the thing they represent.

Now let's try it with a thought you have about yourself — a self-limiting belief, a self-critical label. What's a word or short phrase your mind uses to describe you negatively?

[Client identifies — e.g., 'failure' or 'worthless' or 'broken']

Try repeating it aloud for 30 seconds. Just notice what happens to it as a word.

[Practice]

This is defusion: seeing a word as a word, not as truth. The word 'failure' is just sounds. What you do with your life is not determined by a collection of sounds."

## Practice 3: The Story of My Life

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion, Self-as-Context
**Best Used When**: Client is heavily identified with a particular narrative — especially a limiting or problem-saturated narrative

### Instructions

"Each of us has a story — a narrative we tell ourselves and others about who we are and what our life is about. This story is often quite powerful. It shapes what we do, what we try, and what we think is possible for us.

I'd like to name the story you seem to be telling. Based on what you've shared with me, your story seems to go something like: '[Clinician offers a brief, compassionate summary of the client's central narrative]'

Does that resonate?

Now here's an ACT idea: notice that you just heard your story described — which means there's a you who is hearing it. You are not the story. You are the one who holds the story.

What's it like to hear your story from the outside?

And: what if that story is one story — not the only story, not the definitive story, but one narrative among possibilities?

From the place of the one who holds the story: what story might serve you better right now? What story includes more of who you are?"

## Practice 4: I'm Having the Thought That...

**Duration**: 5–10 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion
**Best Used When**: In-the-moment defusion practice; can be used in any session when a fused thought arises

### Instructions

"Here's one of the simplest and most powerful defusion techniques available. It's a small change in language that makes a significant difference.

Instead of saying: 'I am a failure' — say: 'I'm having the thought that I am a failure.'

Instead of saying: 'This will never get better' — say: 'I'm having the thought that this will never get better.'

Let's practice. Take a thought you've had recently that has been troubling you. State it the way you would naturally — as if it were true.

[Client states thought]

Now add: 'I'm having the thought that...'

[Client repeats with prefix]

What changes? Just notice."

## Practice 5: The Self-as-Author Exercise

**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Self-as-Context, Defusion
**Best Used When**: Client needs to develop perspective on their own narrative; useful for narrative reconstruction work

### Instructions

"I'd like to invite you to step into a particular role: the author of your story.

An author has a different relationship to the story than a character does. The character lives inside the story — they're in it, subject to it. The author stands slightly outside — they can see the whole arc, the themes, the choices, the possibilities.

From the perspective of the author of your life story:

What are the themes that have run through your story so far?

Who are the main characters, and how have they shaped the protagonist (you)?

What chapter are you in now? What was the chapter before?

The interesting thing about being an author: you can see what's happened so far — you can't change that — but you have some say in what the next chapter contains.

What does the author of your story want to write next?

Not what the character fears, or what the plot seems to demand — but what the author, who can see the whole, wants to write."

## Practice 6: The Movie Screen Metaphor

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Self-as-Context, Defusion
**Best Used When**: Client is overwhelmed by thoughts and feelings about a specific situation; needs perspective

### Instructions

"Imagine you're sitting in a movie theater. On the screen in front of you, a film is playing — and the film is your current situation. The characters are you and the people in your life. The scene that's troubling you is playing out on screen.

From your seat in the theater, you can see the whole scene. You can see what the character is feeling, what's happening around them, what led to this moment.

Notice: you're watching from the seat. You're not on the screen. You have a perspective.

From this vantage point — the audience member watching the film — what do you see that the character on screen can't see?

What does the audience member know that the character doesn't know yet?

And what might the audience member want to say to the character in that scene, if they could?"

## Practice 7: Perspective-Taking Visualization

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Self-as-Context, Defusion
**Best Used When**: Client is stuck in a single interpretation of a situation; needs to expand perspective

### Instructions

"I'd like to guide you through a perspective-taking exercise. We're going to look at [a specific situation] through several different sets of eyes.

First: see it through your own eyes, as you have been experiencing it. What do you see, feel, think?

[Pause]

Now: imagine stepping into the perspective of someone else involved in this situation — not to excuse anything, but to understand. What might they be experiencing? What might they believe? What might they fear?

[Pause]

Now: imagine an observer — someone wise, who cares about everyone involved but has no stake in the outcome. Looking at this situation from the outside: what do they see that might not be visible from inside?

[Pause]

Finally: imagine yourself at 80 years old, looking back at this moment. From that vantage, what stands out? What matters? What would you want the current version of you to know?

What shifts, if anything, after moving through these different perspectives?"

## Practice 8: Values-Based Story Revision

**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Defusion, Self-as-Context
**Best Used When**: Integrating defusion and perspective work into narrative transformation; connecting story revision to values direction

### Instructions

"We've done some work on being able to see your story from the outside — as author, as audience member, through different eyes.

Now I'd like to bring values into this.

From the perspective of your values — who you want to be, what you stand for — how would those values describe the protagonist of your story?

Not what the inner critic says. Not what fear says. What do your values see when they look at you?

[Explore — values as a perspective-taking position]

And: what story does the values-perspective want to tell going forward? Not a fantasy story — a real story, grounded in who you are. But one that includes more of the person you're becoming, and less of the constraints of who the old story said you were."

## Integration of Defusion and Perspective-Taking

These practices work best as an integrated sequence:
1. **Establish defusion** (Practices 1–4): Build capacity to observe thoughts
2. **Expand perspective** (Practices 5–7): Use the observing position to access multiple viewpoints
3. **Connect to values** (Practice 8): Use the freed-up perspective to act from values rather than from habitual narrative
