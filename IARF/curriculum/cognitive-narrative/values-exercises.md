# Cognitive-Narrative Dimension: Values Exercises

---

## Overview

The values exercises in the Cognitive-Narrative dimension connect the work of story and thought to the deeper dimension of what matters. Our narratives are not merely cognitive products — they are value-laden. The stories we tell about ourselves encode our assumptions about what is possible, who we are allowed to be, and what matters. By bringing values clarity to narrative work, we transform story revision from a cognitive exercise into a values-aligned act of identity and direction.

These exercises draw from narrative therapy (White & Epston), ACT values and defusion work, positive psychology's strengths-based approach, and post-traumatic growth frameworks.

---

## Exercise 1: The Story I Tell About Myself

**Purpose**: To bring explicit awareness to one's dominant self-narrative; to examine it with curiosity and values-clarity
**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written or verbal reflection

### Instructions

"We all tell ourselves a story about who we are. This story may be explicit or implicit — we may have words for it, or it may simply be the lens through which we see everything.

Let me ask you: what's the main story you tell about yourself?

If you had to describe the central narrative your mind uses to explain your life — why things have turned out the way they have, what kind of person you are, what is and isn't possible for you — what would that story be?

Write or speak it honestly. Don't edit.

[Allow 5–10 minutes]

Now: sit back from what you just wrote or said. You are the person who just told that story. You are not the story itself.

Ask these questions:
1. Where did this story come from? Whose voice is in it?
2. Does this story represent all of who you are — or does it leave things out?
3. What would your values say about this story? Does it tell the story your values would tell?
4. What is missing from this story that is actually true about you?"

## Exercise 2: Rewriting the Protagonist (Strengths-Based Narrative Revision)

**Purpose**: To revise one's self-narrative to include evidence of strengths, competencies, and values-aligned behavior that the dominant story has minimized
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written or guided

### Instructions

"When our dominant story is problem-saturated — organized around difficulty, failure, or limitation — the strengths and competencies we have demonstrated get buried. This exercise excavates them.

Think about your life history — not the chapter headings provided by the dominant story, but the actual events and behaviors.

Now answer these questions:
1. What are the most difficult things you have survived or navigated?
2. In navigating those difficulties, what qualities did you demonstrate — even imperfectly?
3. What moments in your life reveal a different protagonist than the dominant story describes?
4. If a character in a novel did what you have done — what would we say about that person?

Now: write a brief (5–10 sentence) alternative description of the protagonist of your life story. Not an idealized version — a more complete version. A description that includes the difficulties and the strengths, the failures and the responses to failure."

## Exercise 3: The Chapter Titles Exercise

**Purpose**: To bring narrative structure and meaning to one's life history; to identify themes and turning points
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written

### Instructions

"Imagine your life as a book. Each major period of your life is a chapter — perhaps 5–10 chapters so far.

Give each chapter a title. Not just a description ("Age 8–12") but a meaningful title that captures what that period was about — what you were learning, what you were navigating, what was at stake.

[Examples: 'Finding My Footing,' 'Years of Waiting,' 'When Everything Changed,' 'Learning to Stand,' 'The Long Road Back']

Write your chapter titles in order.

Now, looking at your chapter list:
- What themes run through multiple chapters?
- Which chapter do you feel most proud of?
- Which chapter do you most wish had been different?
- What does the arc of your chapters suggest about where you're headed?
- What title would you want for the next chapter?"

## Exercise 4: My Growth Story (Post-Traumatic Growth Narrative)

**Purpose**: To construct a narrative that integrates adversity and growth; to use the PTG framework to organize one's experience
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written or verbal

### Instructions

"Post-traumatic growth research (Tedeschi & Calhoun) identifies five domains in which people often report positive change after adversity: personal strength, new possibilities, relating to others, appreciation of life, and spiritual/existential change.

I'd like to invite you to explore whether any of these growth domains are present in your experience — not as a requirement, and not to minimize what you've suffered, but to see what's there alongside the suffering.

For each domain, reflect:

**Personal strength**: What do you know about your own strength now that you didn't know before the adversity?

**New possibilities**: What paths, values, or possibilities opened up because of — not despite — what you've been through?

**Relating to others**: How has your capacity for connection, empathy, or intimacy changed?

**Appreciation of life**: What do you value now that you didn't fully appreciate before?

**Spiritual/existential change**: How has your understanding of what matters — your sense of meaning or purpose — evolved?

Write a growth story: 'What I have been through, and what it has opened in me...'"

### Facilitator Notes
- This exercise is only appropriate when the client has had adequate time to process the adversity
- Never present growth as the "goal" of trauma — it is an emergent phenomenon, not an obligation
- Acknowledge that growth and grief coexist; the exercise does not require leaving grief behind

## Exercise 5: What My Struggles Reveal About What I Value

**Purpose**: To use the cognitive-narrative history of struggle as a values clarification tool
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual

### Instructions

"What we struggle with most often reveals what we care about most deeply.

If you struggle with boundaries, it may be because you deeply value connection and fear losing it. If you struggle with failure, it may be because you deeply value excellence and contribution. If you struggle with conflict, it may be because you deeply value peace and harmony in relationships.

Our struggles are not random — they follow the contours of our values.

I'd like to explore: what have you struggled with most persistently? And what does each struggle reveal about what you care about?

Complete this for each major struggle:

| My Struggle | What This Struggle Reveals I Value |

Now: knowing that each struggle is connected to a value — how does that change how you hold the struggle? And how does it clarify what you want to move toward?"

## Exercise 6: The Letter to My Future Self

**Purpose**: To project values and growth into the future; to create a forward-looking cognitive-narrative anchor
**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written exercise

### Instructions

"Write a letter to yourself at a point 5 years in the future. Not to the idealized version of yourself — to the real version: someone who has continued to grow, has faced new challenges, has continued to live from your values (imperfectly and courageously).

In your letter:
1. Describe who you hope to be by that point — not in terms of achievements, but in terms of character
2. Share what you want your future self to remember from this period — what matters, what you're learning
3. Offer your future self encouragement for challenges they might face
4. Name the values you are committing to carry forward

[Allow 15 minutes of writing]

Read the letter back. What did your future self need to hear? And is there anything in this letter that your present self needs to hear right now?"

## Exercise 7: Collective Story — Who Came Before Me?

**Purpose**: To connect the individual's narrative to collective and intergenerational context; to access inherited resilience resources
**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; can be adapted for families or groups

### Instructions

"None of our stories begin with us. We are part of a larger story — a family story, a cultural story, an ancestral story.

I'd like to invite you to think about who came before you — parents, grandparents, community members, historical figures who share your identity — and what their stories offer you.

Reflect on:
1. What adversities did the people who came before you face? What did they survive?
2. What qualities, skills, or values did they demonstrate in surviving?
3. What have you inherited from them — not only challenges, but resources, wisdom, and strength?
4. When you face difficulty, whose example can you draw on from your collective story?

Complete this statement: 'The people who came before me survived ____________, and what they modeled for me is ____________. I carry that forward by ____________.'"

### Cultural Considerations
- This exercise is especially resonant for clients from communities with strong collective identities
- For clients with traumatic family or collective histories (genocide, slavery, forced migration), approach with sensitivity — this exercise can surface profound grief alongside resilience
- Honor the complexity of inheritance: we inherit both wounds and wisdom from those who came before
