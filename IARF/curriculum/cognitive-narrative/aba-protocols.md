# Cognitive-Narrative Dimension: ABA Protocols

---

## Overview

The application of ABA principles to cognitive and narrative processes represents an important frontier in behavioral science. While cognitions are not directly observable, their behavioral products — verbal reports, written records, problem-solving behaviors, and narrative productions — are. ABA offers tools for systematically shaping, reinforcing, and generalizing adaptive cognitive behaviors including reframing, flexible thinking, growth mindset language, and strengths-based narrative.

This document presents ABA protocols for the Cognitive-Narrative dimension of The Resilience Atlas™ IARF, adapted for use within a trauma-informed, values-aligned, and psychologically flexible treatment framework.

---

## Protocol 1: Cognitive Restructuring Protocol (Behavioral Approach)

### Purpose
To teach a systematic behavioral procedure for examining and modifying maladaptive thought patterns using a structured thought record.

### Procedure

**Step 1: Thought Identification**
- Teach the client to identify specific automatic thoughts using a thought record (ABC: Antecedent, Belief, Consequence)
- Practice in session across multiple low-stakes examples
- Reinforce identification of specific thoughts (not vague "I felt bad")

**Step 2: Evidence Examination**
- For each identified thought, guide the client through examining evidence:
  - "What evidence supports this thought?"
  - "What evidence contradicts this thought?"
- Reinforce the behavior of generating balanced evidence

**Step 3: Alternative Generation**
- Guide the client in generating at least 2 alternative interpretations
- Reinforce the generation of alternatives without requiring them to replace the original thought
- Use the prompt: "What else might be true?"

**Step 4: Behavioral Experiment (optional)**
- For thoughts that can be tested, design a simple behavioral experiment
- The client gathers real-world data to test the thought
- Review data together; reinforce the experimental behavior regardless of outcome

**Step 5: Application**
- Assign thought record practice as homework (3–5 records per week)
- Review at each session; provide descriptive feedback
- Reinforce completion and quality of thinking

### Mastery Criterion
Client completes a full thought record (identify → evidence → alternative) independently across 3 consecutive examples with ≥80% accuracy.

## Protocol 2: Thought Recording and Analysis (Behavioral Chain)

| Step | Behavior | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Trigger recognition | Notices an emotional or behavioral reaction |
| 2 | Thought identification | Pauses and identifies the automatic thought driving the reaction |
| 3 | Thought recording | Writes the thought down (externalizes it) |
| 4 | Evidence analysis | Writes evidence for and against |
| 5 | Alternative generation | Generates at least one alternative interpretation |
| 6 | Reappraisal rating | Rates distress before and after the record (0–10) |
| 7 | Action selection | Selects a values-consistent response based on the reappraised thought |

Reinforce completion of each step independently; fade prompts systematically.

## Protocol 3: Discrimination Training for Adaptive vs. Maladaptive Thoughts

### Purpose
To teach clients to discriminate between thoughts that support values-aligned functioning (adaptive) and thoughts that undermine it (maladaptive).

### Procedure
1. Present thought examples (clinician-generated or from client's records)
2. Client categorizes as adaptive (supports values-aligned action) or maladaptive (blocks values-aligned action)
3. Reinforce accurate discrimination
4. Progress to real-time discrimination during session discussion
5. Assign in-vivo discrimination practice

### Adaptive vs. Maladaptive Criteria (not good/bad, but functional)

| Thought Characteristic | Adaptive | Maladaptive |
|---|---|---|
| Accuracy | Evidence-based; acknowledges uncertainty | Distorted; catastrophizes; ignores evidence |
| Function | Supports values-aligned action | Maintains avoidance; produces paralysis |
| Flexibility | Allows multiple interpretations | Rigid; all-or-nothing |
| Time orientation | Present-focused; learning-oriented | Ruminative; catastrophic future |

## Protocol 4: Reinforcement of Adaptive Thinking Patterns

| Target Behavior | Reinforcement Approach | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Generating alternative interpretation | Verbal reinforcement: "You just offered a completely different way to see that" | Reinforce the generation, not the specific alternative |
| Identifying cognitive pattern | Acknowledgment: "You caught that catastrophizing thought in real time" | Reinforce awareness |
| Growth mindset language | Descriptive reinforcement: "That's a growth orientation — 'I haven't learned this yet'" | Distinguish from fixed mindset statements |
| Completing thought record | Review and engage with content | Natural reinforcer = insight and reduced distress |
| Perspective-taking attempt | Validation and curiosity: "What did it feel like to try to see it from their perspective?" | Reinforce the attempt, regardless of how well it "worked" |

## Protocol 5: Antecedent Modifications for Cognitive Patterns

| Antecedent Modification | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-session thought record review | Prime cognitive awareness before challenging work | Review last week's records together before new content |
| Growth mindset priming | Shift mindset orientation before learning tasks | Brief reflection: "What's one thing you're getting better at?" |
| Deceleration cue | Interrupt cognitive automaticity | Client-identified physical cue (hand on chest; three breaths) to slow down before reacting |
| Values reminder | Connect cognitive work to values direction | Visual values card on desk during challenging cognitive tasks |
| Question prompts | Replace automatic thought with a question | "Is this thought helping me move in my chosen direction?" |

## Protocol 6: Behavioral Experiments for Testing Beliefs

Behavioral experiments are among the most powerful interventions for modifying cognitive patterns — they provide real-world data that either confirms or disconfirms a belief through direct experience.

### Procedure

**Step 1: Identify the target belief**
- "What do you believe will happen if...?"
- Belief: "If I share my struggles with my colleague, they will think less of me."

**Step 2: Operationalize the experiment**
- What specific behavior will test this belief?
- In what setting? With whom? When?
- What data will you collect?

**Step 3: Predict the outcome**
- "Before the experiment: how strongly do you believe this? (0–100%)"
- Record the specific prediction

**Step 4: Conduct the experiment**
- Client executes the planned behavior
- Records what actually happened

**Step 5: Review and update**
- Compare prediction to outcome
- Revise belief rating: "How strongly do you believe this now?"
- Generate an updated, more evidence-based belief

## Protocol 7: Narrative Skill-Building Protocols

### Purpose
To build the narrative competencies — coherent storytelling, strengths identification, post-traumatic narrative construction — that support resilience through structured practice.

**Narrative Practice 1: Story Structure Teaching**
- Teach clients the basic structure of a coherent life narrative: beginning (origins and context), middle (challenges and responses), present (where they are now), and direction (where they are heading)
- Use narrative scaffolding to support clients whose narratives are fragmentary or incoherent
- Reinforce each structural element as it becomes more robust

**Narrative Practice 2: Re-storying Exercises**
- Select a specific personal story and practice retelling it with different emphases:
  - Emphasizing competence rather than failure
  - Including the context rather than only the individual
  - Including what was learned rather than only what happened
- Reinforce each retelling as an exercise in flexibility

**Narrative Practice 3: Strengths Archaeology**
- Systematically mine the client's personal history for evidence of strength, competence, and values
- Build a "strengths archive" that can be drawn upon as narrative resources
- Reinforce specific identification of strengths with behavioral evidence (not vague compliments)

## Trauma-Informed Cognitive ABA

- **Pace carefully**: Cognitive restructuring with traumatic material requires extensive preparation
- **Validate before reframing**: Always validate the client's reality before offering alternative perspectives
- **Don't rush narrative coherence**: Building a coherent narrative after trauma takes time; honor the fragmentation as meaningful
- **Avoid forced growth narratives**: Post-traumatic growth cannot be prescribed; it emerges from the client's own process
- **Reinforce safety first**: Ensure cognitive work occurs within a safely regulated window
