# Relational-Connective Dimension: Skills Inventory

> *"Connection is why we are here. It is what gives purpose and meaning to our lives."*
> — Brené Brown, *The Gifts of Imperfection*

---

## Overview

The Relational-Connective dimension of the IATLAS addresses one of the most consistently identified protective factors in resilience research: social connection. From Werner and Smith's Kauai study to Southwick and Charney's trauma resilience research, the presence of meaningful, supportive relationships is among the strongest predictors of positive outcomes under adversity.

"Relational" refers to the quality, depth, and bidirectionality of human connections. "Connective" refers to the active behaviors that build, maintain, and repair relationships over time. Together, these dimensions capture both the state of connection and the skills that create and sustain it.

The Relational-Connective dimension recognizes that resilience is inherently social. We survive hardship not only through individual strength but through the webs of relationship that hold us — and through our capacity to both offer and receive support. This skills inventory maps the behavioral competencies that enable individuals to build and sustain those webs within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

---

## Core Skill Domains

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **Social Support Seeking** | The capacity to identify when support is needed, locate appropriate sources of support, and request help effectively |
| **Maintaining Connections** | Behavioral skills for sustaining relationships over time through regular contact, investment, and reciprocity |
| **Communication Skills** | Verbal and non-verbal behaviors that support clear, honest, and empathic exchange in relationships |
| **Boundary-Setting** | The ability to identify and communicate personal limits in relationships in ways that protect both self and connection |
| **Reciprocity & Interdependence** | The capacity to both give and receive support; to experience relationships as mutual rather than one-directional |

## Skills Inventory

### Level 1: Foundational Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Identifying Support Figures** | The ability to name at least one person in one's life who could provide support in a difficult moment | Can name 1–3 people they trust; can distinguish types of support different people offer; does not exclusively identify professional helpers |
| **Basic Help-Requesting** | The ability to ask for help in a direct, clear manner | Asks for help using specific requests rather than indirect hints; accepts help when offered; can tolerate help without shame |
| **Active Listening** | Attending fully to what another person is saying without planning a response, interrupting, or dismissing | Maintains eye contact (culturally appropriate); reflects back what they heard; asks clarifying questions; resists the urge to fix or advise |
| **Expressing Needs Clearly** | The ability to communicate one's own needs in relationships using "I" statements and specific language | States needs directly; avoids accusatory or vague language; tolerates the other person's response to stated needs |
| **Contact Initiation** | The ability to reach out to others proactively rather than waiting to be contacted | Initiates contact at least once per week with support network members; does not rely exclusively on others to maintain connection |
| **Receiving Support Gracefully** | The capacity to accept help, care, and connection from others without deflecting, dismissing, or becoming overwhelmed | Verbally acknowledges support received; expresses appreciation; tolerates vulnerability of being helped |
| **Social Awareness** | Basic reading of social cues, emotional states of others, and relational context | Notices when others are distressed; adjusts behavior based on social context; reads non-verbal cues accurately |
| **Empathic Response** | The ability to respond to others' emotional experiences with recognition and validation rather than advice or judgment | Validates emotional experiences verbally; does not minimize or redirect; communicates understanding without agenda |

---

### Level 2: Intermediate Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Support Network Mapping** | Systematic identification of the breadth, depth, and quality of one's social support network | Can diagram a social network with at least 5 people; identifies functional roles in network; recognizes gaps and strengths |
| **Conflict Navigation** | The capacity to engage with interpersonal conflict in ways that preserve the relationship while addressing the issue | Raises concerns directly rather than avoiding or exploding; uses collaborative problem-solving in conflict; does not personalize disagreements as relationship endings |
| **Appropriate Self-Disclosure** | Sharing personal information at a level that is contextually appropriate and supports relational depth | Calibrates disclosure to relationship context; shares vulnerable information in appropriate relationships; does not overshare with acquaintances or undershare with intimate connections |
| **Boundary Articulation** | The capacity to name and communicate personal limits clearly and respectfully | States limits using "I" language; maintains limits when pushed; re-states limits without escalation; sets limits proactively rather than reactively |
| **Relationship Repair** | The ability to acknowledge harm, apologize, and re-engage after relational rupture | Takes responsibility for own contributions to conflict; makes genuine (non-defensive) apologies; follows through on repair commitments |
| **Reciprocal Support** | Actively offering support to others in one's network, not only receiving | Checks in with others during their difficulties; offers tangible and emotional support; does not become primarily a support-taker in relationships |
| **Community Connection** | Engagement with community groups, shared-identity networks, or collective activities beyond immediate personal relationships | Participates in at least one community activity or group; expresses sense of belonging to a community larger than immediate family |
| **Digital/Social Media Boundaries** | The ability to navigate social media and digital communication in ways that support rather than undermine relational wellbeing | Monitors impact of digital interaction on mood; sets limits on social comparison; uses digital contact to supplement, not replace, in-person connection |

### Level 3: Advanced Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Network Cultivation** | Proactive, strategic development of one's social network for mutual benefit and resilience | Intentionally builds new relationships in valued domains; diversifies network across different life areas; maintains network even when not in immediate need |
| **Mentoring Others** | Providing guidance, support, and wisdom to less experienced individuals navigating difficulty | Demonstrates patient, non-directive mentoring; shares own experiences without dominating; supports mentee's autonomy |
| **Community Leadership** | Taking active roles in community organizations, groups, or initiatives that build collective wellbeing | Holds a role or responsibility in a community group; helps facilitate group functions; models relational values publicly |
| **Cross-Cultural Connection** | The capacity to build authentic connections across cultural, ethnic, linguistic, or identity differences | Seeks to understand rather than assume; acknowledges own cultural positionality; builds genuine relationships with people from different backgrounds |
| **Navigating Rupture and Repair** | The sophisticated capacity to manage and grow from significant relational ruptures | Remains in relationship through significant conflict; initiates repair when others hesitate; uses rupture as an opportunity for deeper connection |
| **Support System Diversification** | Intentionally maintaining diverse types of relationships to prevent support network fragility | Network includes intimate, community, professional, peer, and spiritual support sources; not over-reliant on any single person |
| **Collective Resilience** | Contributing to and benefiting from group or community resilience during shared adversity | Mobilizes community resources during shared difficulty; facilitates mutual support in group contexts; articulates value of collective response |
| **Network Advocacy** | Speaking up for the relational needs of others; challenging relational isolation in community systems | Advocates for isolated individuals; challenges stigma around help-seeking; creates welcoming environments for connection |

## Cross-Dimensional Connections

| Connected Dimension | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Agentic-Generative** | Help-seeking is an agentic skill; mentoring others is both relational and agentic; initiative is required for contact initiation |
| **Spiritual-Reflective** | Communities of meaning are both relational and spiritual; forgiveness is a relational values practice; shared ritual builds relational cohesion |
| **Emotional-Adaptive** | Emotional expression and regulation are foundational to authentic connection; empathic response requires emotional attunement |
| **Somatic-Regulative** | Co-regulation — the ability of bodies to regulate together — is the foundation of early attachment; somatic safety enables relational vulnerability |
| **Cognitive-Narrative** | Social support is often delivered through narrative (witnessing, bearing witness); cognitive narratives about relationships shape relational behavior |

## Assessment Considerations

Before beginning relational-connective skills work, assess:

1. **Current network size and quality**: How many people does the client have access to? What is the quality of those relationships?
2. **Attachment history**: Early attachment experiences significantly shape adult relational patterns. Consider attachment style in skills targeting.
3. **Trauma history in relationships**: Many clients have experienced relational trauma (abuse, neglect, abandonment). Safety and pacing are critical.
4. **Cultural context**: The meaning, forms, and expectations of relationships vary enormously across cultural contexts.
5. **Current isolation levels**: Assess for social isolation as a clinical risk factor.
6. **Barriers to connection**: Fear, shame, perceived burdensomeness, and trauma history are common barriers.

## Cultural and Contextual Factors

The Relational-Connective dimension is perhaps the most culturally shaped of all six IATLAS dimensions. Practitioners must attend carefully to cultural context in all aspects of relational skills work.

**Key considerations:**

- **Collectivistic cultures**: In many cultural contexts, relationships are the primary unit of meaning and identity. Skills work should amplify existing relational strengths, not impose individualistic frameworks.
- **Family structures**: Extended family, chosen family, and non-nuclear family structures are all valid and should be honored in network mapping.
- **Gender norms**: Social norms around help-seeking, emotional expression, and reciprocity vary by gender. Avoid gender-normative assumptions.
- **Immigration and diaspora contexts**: Clients navigating immigration have often experienced significant relational loss and may be rebuilding networks in new cultural contexts.
- **LGBTQ+ communities**: Chosen family networks may be more important than biological family; heteronormative relational frameworks should be actively avoided.
- **Disability and accessibility**: Ensure that "connection" strategies are accessible to clients with physical, sensory, or cognitive disabilities.
