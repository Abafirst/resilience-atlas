# Relational-Connective Dimension: ABA Protocols

---

## Overview

Applied Behavior Analysis offers robust, evidence-based approaches to building social and relational skills. ABA's emphasis on observable behavior, systematic instruction, and reinforcement makes it particularly well-suited to the skill-building aspects of the Relational-Connective dimension. The protocols in this document adapt core ABA methodology for use in trauma-informed, values-aligned relational resilience work within The Resilience Atlas™ IATLAS.

Relational behaviors are shaped by the same principles as all behavior: they respond to antecedents, occur in functional contexts, and are maintained or extinguished by consequences. ABA provides clinicians with a systematic toolkit for assessing the current state of a client's social repertoire and building new relational behaviors with precision and care.

All protocols should be adapted to the client's specific relational history, cultural context, and attachment patterns.

---

## Core ABA Principles in the Relational Context

- **Social reinforcement**: Relational behaviors are largely maintained by social reinforcers (attention, affection, reciprocity). Understanding the client's social reinforcement history is essential.
- **Modeling**: Observational learning of social behavior is highly effective; clinicians and group settings are powerful modeling environments.
- **Behavioral rehearsal**: Practicing social behaviors in low-stakes conditions prepares for generalization to natural environments.
- **Chaining**: Complex social interactions can be analyzed as behavioral chains with discrete, teachable links.
- **Generalization**: Social skills must generalize across partners, settings, and emotional states to be genuinely useful.

## Protocol 1: Social Skills Training

### Purpose
To systematically build a client's repertoire of relational behaviors through structured instruction, modeling, rehearsal, feedback, and reinforcement.

### Procedure

**Step 1: Social Skills Assessment**
- Identify current strengths and deficits in the client's social repertoire
- Use behavioral observation, client report, and standardized assessment (see measurement.md)
- Prioritize skill targets based on functional relevance and values alignment

**Step 2: Skill Selection and Goal Setting**
- Collaboratively select 1–3 social skills targets per treatment phase
- Define each skill in observable, behavioral terms
- Establish a measurable mastery criterion for each target

**Step 3: Instruction**
- Provide a clear rationale for the skill (why this skill matters for connection)
- Describe the skill in specific behavioral terms (what it looks, sounds, and feels like)
- Use social stories, psychoeducation, or written instructions as appropriate

**Step 4: Modeling**
- Demonstrate the skill — either live (clinician), via video modeling, or through description of examples
- Model both positive examples and non-examples (contrast helps discrimination learning)
- Discuss what was observed: "What did you notice about how I [did the skill]?"

**Step 5: Behavioral Rehearsal / Role Play**
- Client practices the skill in a structured role-play with the clinician
- Use graduated scenarios from low to high difficulty
- Provide specific, descriptive feedback after each rehearsal

**Step 6: Feedback and Reinforcement**
- Reinforce any approximation toward the target skill
- Provide corrective feedback in a specific, non-shaming manner
- Highlight improvement over previous attempts

**Step 7: Natural Environment Practice**
- Assign specific in-vivo social skill practice for the coming week
- Use implementation intentions ("When I see [person], I will [skill]")
- Review at next session; reinforce completion and problem-solve barriers

**Step 8: Generalization Promotion**
- Identify multiple relevant social partners and contexts for the skill
- Vary practice scenarios to include different social partners, settings, and emotional conditions
- Monitor generalization data over time

## Protocol 2: Modeling and Imitation Training

### Video Modeling Protocol

**Purpose**: To use video examples of skilled relational behavior as a modeling stimulus, particularly effective for clients who benefit from concrete demonstration.

**Procedure**:
1. Select or create a brief video (2–5 minutes) demonstrating the target relational behavior in a realistic, culturally appropriate context
2. Watch the video together with the client
3. Discuss: "What did the person do? What happened as a result?"
4. Practice the modeled behavior via role-play
5. Reinforce approximations; compare to the model positively
6. Assign real-world practice with feedback at next session

### In-Vivo Modeling Protocol

**Purpose**: To model target social behaviors in the natural environment or session context.

**Procedure**:
1. Identify a naturally occurring opportunity to demonstrate the target skill (in session or in a supported community setting)
2. Perform the skill with clear intention while the client observes
3. Debrief: "What did you notice? What would you like to try?"
4. Client attempts the behavior; clinician provides immediate feedback

## Protocol 3: Prompting Hierarchy for Social Initiation

The following prompt hierarchy supports clients in learning to initiate social contact — one of the most avoidance-prone relational behaviors. Prompts are faded systematically as the behavior becomes more independent.

| Prompt Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **P5 – Full Physical** | Clinician physically guides the behavior | (Rarely appropriate for adults; used with caution) |
| **P4 – Partial Physical** | Light physical cue (e.g., gentle touch on arm to cue action) | Touch on shoulder as prompt to move toward social target |
| **P3 – Full Verbal** | Complete verbal instruction describing what to do | "Walk over to your friend and say, 'Hey, how are you?'" |
| **P2 – Partial Verbal** | Abbreviated or incomplete verbal cue | "Go ahead and say..." or "You could..." |
| **P1 – Gestural** | Non-verbal gesture cuing the behavior | Nodding head toward person; pointing |
| **P0 – Independent** | No prompt; client initiates independently | Unprompted social initiation |

**Fading procedure**: Begin at the least intrusive prompt level that results in correct behavior. Fade prompts systematically over trials as the behavior strengthens. Always reinforce independent (P0) responding most richly.

## Protocol 4: Social Reinforcement Protocols

Understanding what social consequences function as reinforcers for a given client is essential. Social reinforcement schedules should mirror natural relationship dynamics to support generalization.

| Social Behavior | Reinforcement Type | Schedule | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initiating contact | Social praise + brief conversation | FR1 initially; fade to VR | Natural consequence = reciprocal engagement |
| Active listening | Reflective acknowledgment by clinician | FR1 → natural | Natural consequence = speaker continues |
| Expressing a need | Collaborative response to need | FR1 | Natural consequence = need met |
| Offering support | Gratitude + reciprocal offer | Natural/intermittent | Avoid over-praising; maintain naturalness |
| Setting a boundary | Calm clinician acceptance | FR1 initially | Natural consequence = boundary respected |
| Conflict repair | Acknowledgment + forward movement | FR1 → natural | Natural consequence = relationship repair |

## Protocol 5: Role-Play and Behavioral Rehearsal

### Purpose
To provide structured practice of social skills in a controlled, feedback-rich environment prior to real-world application.

### Procedure
1. **Set the scene**: Describe the social scenario clearly (who, where, what situation)
2. **Assign roles**: Clinician plays relational partner; client plays themselves
3. **Begin the role-play**: Use naturalistic dialogue and behavior
4. **Stop and feedback at key moments**: Use a "freeze" signal to pause and discuss specific moments
5. **Replay**: Return to start and replay with modification
6. **Increase difficulty**: Progress to more challenging scenarios as simpler ones are mastered
7. **Debrief**: Discuss what felt natural, what was difficult, and what to try next week

### Sample Role-Play Scenarios (Progressive Difficulty)

| Difficulty | Scenario |
|---|---|
| Low | Greeting a familiar acquaintance at a community event |
| Low-Moderate | Asking a friend if they have time to talk |
| Moderate | Expressing that you need support after a hard week |
| Moderate-High | Setting a limit with someone who regularly oversteps |
| High | Having a conflict conversation with someone important |
| High | Repairing a relationship after you caused harm |

## Protocol 6: Functional Communication Training for Relational Needs

FCT adapts for relational use by identifying the communication function underlying maladaptive relational behaviors (e.g., withdrawal, aggression, passive-aggressive behavior) and replacing them with functionally equivalent, adaptive communicative behaviors.

**Example FCT Analysis:**

| Maladaptive Behavior | Function | Replacement Behavior | Teaching Method |
|---|---|---|---|
| Withdrawing from conversations | Escape from vulnerability | "I need a few minutes before I can talk about this" | Direct instruction + rehearsal |
| Explosive outburst during conflict | Access to attention; escape from perceived dismissal | "I feel like I'm not being heard — can we pause?" | Shaping + role-play |
| Excessive reassurance-seeking | Reduce anxiety; access validation | "I'm feeling anxious about this — can I share it with you?" | Modeling + rehearsal |
| Isolation after distress | Escape from activation | Reaching out to a support person rather than isolating | Behavioral prescription + reinforcement |

## Protocol 7: Generalization Across Social Contexts

Social skills practiced in the clinical setting must transfer to the full range of social contexts a client encounters.

**Generalization strategies:**
- Train with multiple social partners where possible (involve peers, family, or community members in supervised settings)
- Practice across settings: in office, in community, via phone/video
- Practice under varied emotional conditions: when rested and when tired; when calm and when mildly stressed
- Use homework assignments systematically to promote real-world generalization
- Track generalization data separately from acquisition data

## Special Populations Considerations

- **Autism Spectrum Disorder**: Social skills deficits are a core diagnostic feature. Use structured ABA social skills programs (e.g., PEERS curriculum) as a base and adapt for resilience context. [Research citation needed]
- **Social anxiety**: Graduated exposure hierarchy paired with social skills training; ensure skills deficits are distinguished from performance deficits.
- **Intellectual disability**: Simplify instruction; use more concrete modeling; lengthen mastery timelines; involve natural support partners in training.
- **Older adults**: Adjust scenarios for age-relevant social contexts; address grief-related network loss; build digital literacy for maintaining connection.

## Trauma-Informed Relationship Skill Building

Working with relational skills after relational trauma requires particular care:

- **Safety first**: Ensure the therapeutic relationship models safety and predictability before practicing relational vulnerability
- **Avoid forced disclosure**: Clients choose what to share and with whom
- **Validate relational caution**: Difficulty trusting others after betrayal trauma is adaptive, not pathological
- **Build slowly**: Gradual, step-wise approach to social risk; honor the client's pace
- **Address shame explicitly**: Shame is often the primary barrier to help-seeking and relational vulnerability
- **Use ACT acceptance work**: Accept the fear while practicing the behavior (see act-micropractices.md)

## Integration with ACT

ABA social skills training and ACT work together powerfully in the Relational-Connective dimension:

- ACT **values work** clarifies why connection matters → ABA **skill training** builds how to connect
- ACT **defusion** addresses cognitive barriers to social engagement (e.g., "People always let me down") → ABA **rehearsal** builds new relational behavioral history
- ACT **acceptance** of vulnerability and loneliness → ABA **behavioral prescription** of contact initiation despite discomfort
- ACT **self-as-context** provides relational grounding → ABA **communication training** provides relational skill
