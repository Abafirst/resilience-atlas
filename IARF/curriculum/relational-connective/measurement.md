# Relational-Connective Dimension: Measurement & Monitoring

---

## Overview

Measuring relational wellbeing requires attending to multiple levels: the frequency of social contact, the quality of connections, the breadth and resilience of the support network, and the behavioral skills that build and sustain relationships. This document provides a comprehensive measurement framework for the Relational-Connective dimension of the IATLAS.

Relational measurement is inherently more complex than behavioral frequency counts alone. Quality, reciprocity, and felt experience of connection must be captured alongside observable contact behaviors. The measurement tools in this document integrate behavioral data with subjective quality ratings and standardized assessment to provide a complete clinical picture.

---

## Measurement Philosophy

Relational measurement in the IATLAS is:
- **Multi-level**: Addresses network structure, contact frequency, quality, and skill development
- **Collaborative**: Clients understand what is being measured and contribute to interpreting results
- **Culturally humble**: Measurement tools are applied with awareness of cultural definitions of connection and support
- **Strengths-visible**: Tools are designed to reveal relational strengths, not only deficits
- **Sensitive to grief**: Measurement acknowledges that network changes (through loss, estrangement, or geographic change) affect relational data

## Connection Quality Ratings

Contact frequency alone is insufficient — quality matters more than quantity for wellbeing and resilience outcomes.

### Connection Quality Scale (IATLAS Relational)

*Instructions: Rate the following statements about your most important relationships from 0 (not true at all) to 10 (completely true).*

| Item | Rating (0–10) |
|---|---|
| 1. I have at least one person I can be completely honest with. | |
| 2. When I'm in distress, I have someone I can contact. | |
| 3. My relationships feel genuinely two-way (not one-sided). | |
| 4. I feel seen and understood in my closest relationships. | |
| 5. I have at least one relationship where I feel I belong. | |
| 6. The people closest to me support my growth and values. | |
| 7. I can receive care from others without excessive discomfort. | |
| 8. I feel I contribute meaningfully to others in my network. | |
| 9. I have at least one relationship I am actively investing in. | |
| 10. I feel less alone than I did [3/6/12] months ago. | |

**Scoring**: Total out of 100. Scores below 40 indicate significant relational difficulties warranting clinical priority attention.

## Support Utilization Measures

Support utilization — whether clients actually access available support — is a critical behavioral measure in the Relational-Connective dimension.

| Measure | Recording Method | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Help-seeking instances (unsolicited) | Event recording by client | Weekly |
| Support accessed during distress | Post-crisis behavioral review | As events occur |
| Scheduled social contact kept | Ratio of kept to planned contacts | Weekly |
| Support offered to others | Client report | Weekly |
| Network accessed across multiple members | Diversity index of contacts | Monthly |

|---|---|---|

## Network Mapping as Measurement Tool

Network mapping — visually diagramming the client's social network — serves both therapeutic and measurement functions. Conducted at baseline and at regular intervals, it provides:

- **Network size**: Number of people in the network
- **Network diversity**: Breadth across relationship types (family, friends, colleagues, community)
- **Connection quality**: Strength and bidirectionality of connections
- **Network resilience**: Whether the network is over-reliant on one person
- **Network change over time**: Growth, loss, or restructuring of connections

### Network Map Instructions

Draw three concentric circles labeled "Inner Circle" (most intimate, 1–3 people), "Middle Circle" (important but less intimate, 4–10 people), and "Outer Circle" (broader community, acquaintances). Place each person in the network in the appropriate circle and draw lines indicating direction of support (arrows toward client = support received; arrows away from client = support given; bidirectional arrows = mutual support).

Measure at baseline and every 3–4 months. Track changes in network size, density, and diversity as outcome metrics.

## Progress Indicators

### Short-Term Indicators (4–8 Weeks)

- Increase in weekly contact log total from baseline
- At least 1 new unsolicited help-seeking behavior per week
- Connection Quality Scale total increases ≥5 points from baseline
- Completes at least 2 Acts of Connection weekly commitments
- Reports reduced loneliness (UCLA Loneliness Scale score decreases ≥5 points)
- Demonstrates active listening skills in session role-play (≥70%)

### Medium-Term Indicators (3–6 Months)

- Network map shows at least 2 new connections added
- Contact initiation ratio ≥50% client-initiated (vs. purely reactive)
- UCLA Loneliness Scale score in normative range (below clinical threshold)
- Successfully navigates at least 1 conflict using learned skills (client-reported)
- Demonstrates boundary articulation in at least 2 real-world situations
- Reciprocal support established with at least 2 network members
- MOS-SSS scores show clinically meaningful improvement

### Long-Term Indicators (6–12 Months)

- Stable, diverse network of at least 5 meaningful relationships
- Reports subjective sense of belonging (≥7 on 10-point scale) consistently
- Initiates and sustains at least 1 new community connection
- Demonstrates relationship repair after significant rupture
- Acts as a support resource for others (not only a support-seeker)
- Maintains connection during periods of stress without withdrawal to isolation

## Relational-Specific Clinical Decision Points

The following indicators should prompt immediate clinical review:

- ☐ Client reports zero meaningful social contact for 2+ consecutive weeks → Assess for clinical depression, social phobia, or trauma response; consider crisis support planning
- ☐ UCLA Loneliness Scale score in severe range (>50) at any assessment point → Prioritize social isolation intervention; consider group-based delivery
- ☐ Client reports increasing reliance on clinician as sole supportive relationship → Address therapeutic boundaries; accelerate natural network development
- ☐ Significant network loss identified (death, estrangement, geographic move) → Reprioritize network rebuilding; provide grief support
- ☐ Client describes a relationship characterized by coercion, control, or violence → Assess for intimate partner violence or relational abuse; follow relevant safety protocols
- ☐ Contact initiation remains at zero despite repeated intervention → Conduct functional assessment of avoidance; consider graduated exposure; assess for severe social anxiety requiring specialized treatment
