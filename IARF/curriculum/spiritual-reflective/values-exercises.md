# Spiritual-Reflective Dimension: Values Exercises

---

## Overview

The values exercises in the Spiritual-Reflective dimension invite clients into the deepest layer of the IATLAS framework — the exploration of ultimate meaning, sacred priorities, and purposeful contribution. These exercises are designed to be broad enough to serve clients from any tradition (or no tradition) while being rich enough to facilitate genuine transformation.

These exercises work best when approached with unhurried attention. Unlike skills-based exercises in other dimensions, the Spiritual-Reflective values exercises often require space for silence, emergence, and surprise. Practitioners are encouraged to slow down, be curious, and allow these exercises to unfold without a predetermined endpoint.

## Exercise 1: Sacred Values Mapping

**Purpose**: To identify which values the client considers sacred — non-negotiable, fundamental to who they are
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; quiet environment preferred

### Instructions

"Not all values are equal. Some are preferences — nice to have, but negotiable. Others are sacred — non-negotiable, fundamental to who you are. If violated, they create a sense of profound wrongness. If honored, they create a sense of deep rightness.

I'd like to help you identify your sacred values — the ones that are truly inviolable for you.

Read through the following value domains and for each one, ask yourself: 'Is there a value here that I would defend even at personal cost? That I have organized my life around, or wish I had?'"

### Sacred Values Mapping Table

| Life Domain | Possible Sacred Values | Your Sacred Value(s) in This Domain | How Well-Honored (1–10) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Relationships | Loyalty, honesty, love, care, presence | | |
| Work/Vocation | Integrity, excellence, service, creativity | | |
| Community | Justice, contribution, fairness, solidarity | | |
| Spirituality | Faith, reverence, prayer, discernment, truth | | |
| Personal life | Authenticity, freedom, growth, simplicity | | |
| Toward suffering | Compassion, courage, meaning-making, hope | | |
| Toward the future | Legacy, stewardship, generativity, hope | | |

"Look across your map: which values appeared most consistently? Which are most poorly honored in your current life? These represent your deepest spiritual-reflective priorities."

### Facilitator Notes
- Be alert to values that are claimed but not lived — gently explore the gap
- Distinguish between aspirational values (who I want to be) and operative values (who I actually am)
- Sacred values mapping can be done across religious or secular frameworks — adapt language accordingly

## Exercise 2: The Meaning Inventory

**Purpose**: To identify current sources of meaning and areas where meaning is lacking
**Duration**: 25–35 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written reflection

### Instructions

"Meaning is not only found in grand moments — it lives in the everyday fabric of how we spend our time and attention. This exercise helps you take stock of where meaning is currently present in your life and where it is absent.

Rate each area below from 0 (no meaning here currently) to 10 (deeply meaningful to me right now):"

|---|---|---|

## Exercise 3: Purpose Statement Development

**Purpose**: To construct a personal purpose statement — a compass-level articulation of what one is for
**Duration**: 30–45 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; can be refined across multiple sessions

### Instructions

"A purpose statement is different from a mission statement. It's not about what you do — it's about why you do it, and what you are ultimately for.

Let's work toward a purpose statement that is:
- True: genuinely yours, not inherited or performed
- Brief: one or two sentences
- Directional: it points toward action
- Inclusive: it covers multiple domains of your life

To develop it, let's work through several prompts:

**Prompt 1**: What problem in the world most makes you want to do something about it?

**Prompt 2**: What natural abilities or gifts do you find yourself using most fully when you're at your best?

**Prompt 3**: What do people most come to you for? What do you offer that others seem to need?

**Prompt 4**: If you could write one sentence on your gravestone — not about what you achieved but about who you were — what would it say?

Now, from these responses, try drafting a purpose statement: 'I am here to ___________'"

### Facilitator Notes
- Purpose statements are not permanent — they evolve
- Some clients will find this exercise liberating; others may find it anxiety-provoking (particularly if they feel purposeless)
- For clients in crisis of meaning, this exercise is often best saved for later in treatment when some stabilization has occurred
- Celebrate imperfect drafts — a draft purpose statement is better than no purpose statement

## Exercise 4: The Legacy Letter

**Purpose**: To articulate what the client wants to leave behind; to connect present behavior to long-range contribution
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written exercise

### Instructions

"A legacy letter is a letter you write — not to read at a specific event, but to clarify for yourself what you are building. It is written to the people, communities, or future world you most care about.

Your legacy letter addresses:
1. What you have learned that you want to pass on
2. What you hope you have contributed
3. What you want the people you love to know about who you are
4. What you hope endures after you

You do not have to share this letter with anyone. Write it freely, as if to yourself and the people who will carry your influence forward."

### Prompts to Support Letter Writing

- "The most important thing I have learned in my life is..."
- "What I hope I gave to the people I love is..."
- "I believe in these things above all..."
- "If there is one thing I want to be remembered for, it is..."
- "The work I am most proud of is..."
- "What I want the world to carry forward is..."

## Exercise 5: Spiritual Autobiography (Brief Version)

**Purpose**: To construct a narrative account of the client's spiritual and meaning-making journey; to identify formative experiences and enduring themes
**Duration**: 45–60 minutes (may span 2 sessions)
**Modality**: Individual; verbal or written

### Instructions

"A spiritual autobiography is not a religious document — it's the story of your inner life: how you have made meaning, what you have believed and why, how your sense of purpose has evolved, and what has shaped your deepest values.

I'd like to invite you to tell me — or write — your spiritual autobiography in three chapters:

**Chapter 1: Formation** (earliest to late adolescence)
- What was the meaning-making world of your childhood?
- What did you believe about why you were here?
- What was sacred or precious in your early world?

**Chapter 2: Quest and Crisis** (adulthood — challenges to and revisions of meaning)
- When did your early meaning-making fail or be challenged?
- What crises, losses, or questions destabilized your sense of purpose?
- What did you find — or seek — in the absence of what you had?

**Chapter 3: Integration** (where you are now)
- What do you believe now about what matters?
- How has adversity shaped your meaning?
- What endures from your journey?"

## Exercise 6: What Gives You Hope

**Purpose**: To identify sources of hope as a spiritual and psychological resilience resource
**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**Modality**: Individual or group

### Instructions

"Hope is not wishful thinking — it is a values-grounded orientation toward the future. Research shows that hope (the belief that a better future is possible and that one has paths toward it) is a significant resilience factor.

I'd like you to reflect honestly: what gives you hope?

Not what you think should give you hope — but what actually does, even a little. It might be large or small, personal or communal, traditional or unexpected.

Complete these prompts:

- 'When I think about my own life, what gives me hope is...'
- 'When I think about the people I love, what gives me hope is...'
- 'When I look at the world, what gives me hope is...'
- 'One thing I have witnessed — in history, in community, in nature — that gives me hope is...'"

### Debrief Questions
- "What does this list tell you about what you value?"
- "How do you keep contact with these sources of hope when things are hard?"
- "Is there one source of hope you'd like to make more present in your daily life?"

## Exercise 7: Transcendent Moments Inventory

**Purpose**: To identify past experiences of transcendence, awe, or profound meaning that can be drawn on as resilience resources
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written inventory

### Instructions

"Transcendent moments are those rare experiences when ordinary boundaries seem to dissolve — when we feel connected to something larger than ourselves, when life's meaning seems suddenly clear, when beauty or wonder pierces through the ordinary. They may be spiritual, aesthetic, relational, or natural.

Research shows that transcendent or peak experiences often serve as touchstones — moments we can return to when we need to remember what is real and what matters.

Identify 3–5 transcendent moments in your life. For each one, describe:"

| Moment Description | Context (Where, When, With Whom) | What Dissolved (What ordinary concerns fell away?) | What Remained (What felt ultimately real or important?) | What This Tells Me About What I Value |

"Look across your moments: what themes appear? What conditions or experiences most reliably give you access to what matters? How can you create those conditions more intentionally?"

## Diverse Spiritual and Secular Adaptations

| Exercise | Religious Adaptation | Secular Adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| Sacred Values Mapping | Include theological virtues, commandments, spiritual vows | Include secular ethical commitments (justice, compassion, truth) |
| Meaning Inventory | Frame in terms of calling and stewardship | Frame in terms of contribution and flourishing |
| Purpose Statement | Can include language of calling, vocation, mission | Use language of contribution, impact, legacy |
| Legacy Letter | May include blessings, prayers, religious commitments | Focus on relationship, values transmission, contribution |
| Spiritual Autobiography | Include faith journey, conversion, doubt, renewal | Include philosophical journey, loss of belief, humanistic affirmations |
| What Gives You Hope | Can include faith, prayer, divine promise | Can include evidence, community, history, human resilience |
| Transcendent Moments | May include religious experiences, prayer, encounters with the sacred | May include aesthetic experiences, nature, profound love, art |
