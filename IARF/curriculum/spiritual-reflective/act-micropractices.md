# Spiritual-Reflective Dimension: ACT Micropractices

---

## ACT and Meaning: Overview

Acceptance & Commitment Therapy occupies a unique position in the landscape of meaning-focused approaches. Like Frankl's logotherapy, ACT is deeply concerned with values and purpose. Unlike logotherapy, ACT holds these concerns within a behavioral framework and does not prescribe any particular meaning or spiritual content. ACT's contribution to the Spiritual-Reflective dimension is its process technology: tools for clarifying what matters, accepting what is, and acting from one's deepest values even under conditions of profound uncertainty.

The ACT hexaflex offers particular gifts for spiritual work:
- **Values clarification** as the spiritual work of discernment
- **Acceptance** as the spiritual practice of surrender
- **Defusion** as the contemplative practice of non-attachment to thought
- **Present-moment awareness** as the contemplative practice of presence
- **Self-as-context** as the spiritual experience of a witnessing, transcendent self
- **Committed action** as the spiritual practice of integrity — living what one believes

The practices in this document draw on these connections, offering clinicians and clients tools that are both psychologically sound and spiritually resonant, accessible across secular and religious traditions.

## Values Work as Spiritual Practice

In many spiritual traditions, the work of discernment — discovering one's true calling, identifying what is sacred, clarifying one's purpose — is the central spiritual task. ACT's values work is a secular parallel to this ancient practice.

When facilitating values work in the Spiritual-Reflective dimension, practitioners may choose to use language that bridges psychological and spiritual frames:
- "Values" / "What matters most" / "Sacred priorities" / "Calling" / "What you're for"
- "Committed action" / "Living with integrity" / "Walking the talk" / "Faithfulness"
- "Psychological flexibility" / "Equanimity" / "Groundedness" / "Resilience"

Always use the client's own language and tradition as the guide.

## Practice 1: The Values Excavation (Deep Version)

**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Beginning values work in the Spiritual-Reflective dimension; client has not deeply examined what ultimately matters

### Instructions

"I'd like to take some time to go deeper than the surface of what we usually call 'values.' We're going to explore what is ultimately important to you — what you'd call sacred, precious, or essential.

Find a comfortable position. Allow your eyes to close or soften your gaze. Take a few slow, deep breaths.

Bring to mind a moment in your life when you knew — deeply, clearly — what matters. It might have been a moment of joy, of grief, of love, or of quiet clarity. A moment when the noise of everyday life fell away and something true remained.

[Pause 45 seconds]

Stay with that moment. What was present? What do you know from that moment about what is ultimately important to you?

[Pause 30 seconds]

Now imagine that you could stand at the end of your life and look back across everything you've experienced, everyone you've loved, everything you've given and received.

From that vantage point: what mattered? What do you find yourself grateful for? What were you for?

[Pause 45 seconds]

Let those insights settle. Don't analyze — just notice what's there.

Gently return your attention to the room. Open your eyes when you're ready."

### Debrief
- "What did you discover or rediscover?"
- "Is there a word or phrase that captures what came up — what is ultimately important to you?"
- "How present is this in your current daily life?"

## Practice 2: Contact with What Matters Most

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Client is disconnected from meaning; life feels hollow or empty; depression has obscured purpose

### Instructions

"Sometimes the demands of daily life — its noise, its urgency, its to-do lists — can bury what matters most. This exercise is about clearing away that covering and making direct contact with what's underneath.

Take a moment to settle. Breathe.

Now I'd like you to bring to mind one thing — just one — that you genuinely care about. It could be a person, a value, a calling, a creative expression, a community. Something that, when you think about it, produces a recognizable response inside you — warmth, longing, vitality.

[Pause 30 seconds]

Stay with it. Don't analyze why it matters. Just be with the fact that it does.

Notice what's in your body as you hold this in mind. This is what contact with what matters feels like.

This experience is available to you at any time. Even in the hardest moments. Even in the emptiest ones.

What's one way you can make contact with this today — in some small, concrete way?"

## Practice 3: Acceptance of Existential Uncertainty

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Acceptance, Defusion, Values
**Best Used When**: Client is in existential crisis; struggling with unanswerable questions; certainty is not available

### Instructions

"One of the most honest things we can say about spiritual and existential life is that there is a lot we don't know. We don't know what happens after death. We don't know if there's ultimate meaning to suffering. We don't know if there's a God, or what God is like, or whether our lives add up to something larger.

For some people, this uncertainty is terrifying. For others, it is freeing. In ACT, we work with uncertainty by accepting it rather than fighting it — and finding out what's possible from that place of acceptance.

I'd like to ask you: what existential uncertainty do you most struggle with?

[Client identifies]

Now: what would it mean to hold that question with openness rather than needing to answer it? To say, 'I don't know — and I can live from my values anyway.'

The mystics of many traditions have called this 'learned ignorance' or 'not-knowing' — a posture of openness to reality that precedes genuine wisdom. What would it be like to adopt that posture toward your unanswered question?"

## Practice 4: Present-Moment Sacred Awareness

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Client is dissociated from present experience; reports life feeling meaningless or mechanical

### Instructions (Guided Awareness Exercise)

"Let's slow down for a moment and practice something that contemplative traditions across the world have valued: bringing full attention to the present moment as it is.

Close your eyes. Notice the temperature of the air in the room. Notice the sounds around you — far sounds, near sounds. Notice the weight of your body in the chair.

Now notice that you are here, in this moment, alive. Whatever is happening in your life — this moment is real. Your breath is real. The light in the room is real. Your heartbeat is real.

Some traditions describe this kind of attention as a form of prayer — complete presence to what is. Others call it mindfulness. Whatever we call it, it is a form of reverence — attending fully to reality as a gift.

[30 seconds of silence]

Is there something in this moment — small, ordinary — that carries a hint of significance? A quality of light, a sound, the fact of being alive?

Open your eyes. Carry this quality of attention into the rest of your day."

## Practice 5: Defusion from Meaning-Blocking Thoughts

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion
**Best Used When**: Nihilistic, despairing, or cynical thoughts are blocking engagement with meaning and purpose

### Common Meaning-Blocking Thoughts and Defusion Approaches

| Meaning-Blocking Thought | Defusion Response |
|---|---|
| "Nothing really matters in the long run" | "I notice my mind is in philosophy mode. What's available to matter right now, in this day?" |
| "My life is too ordinary to have real purpose" | "There goes the comparison narrative again. Ordinary lives are where most meaning happens." |
| "I've suffered too much for life to mean anything" | "My mind is telling me that suffering cancels meaning. Is that true? Or is it a rule my mind made?" |
| "Finding meaning is naive and childish" | "Let me unhook from that thought for a moment. From a place of not-believing-that... what matters?" |
| "I'm too damaged for this to apply to me" | "Thank you, mind. I hear you. And I'm going to explore this anyway." |

## Practice 6: The Larger Context Exercise

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Self-as-Context, Transcendent Perspective
**Best Used When**: Client is trapped in the narrow view of current suffering; catastrophizing; experiencing existential claustrophobia

### Instructions

"When we're in pain, our awareness contracts. The problem becomes everything. This exercise is about expanding our view — not to deny the pain, but to hold it within a larger context.

Close your eyes. Picture yourself as you are right now — sitting in this chair, in this room.

Now imagine the view expanding — you see the building you're in, then the street, then the city. Continue expanding: the country, the continent, the hemisphere, the earth from space — a small, brilliant sphere in the dark.

From this perspective, you are one life among billions, on one planet among trillions, in one moment among billions of years. Hold that perspective.

Now zoom back in — all the way back to this room, this chair, this moment.

What does the contrast between the vast view and this moment reveal? In the context of everything, what is this problem? And in the context of this moment, right now — what matters most?

Some people find this exercise humbling. Others find it liberating. What did you find?"

## Practice 7: Gratitude as ACT Practice

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness, Values
**Best Used When**: Client is contracted around loss and scarcity; gratitude as a values-based practice, not a toxic positivity exercise

### Instructions

"Gratitude in ACT is different from forced positivity. We're not trying to minimize difficulty or pretend everything is fine. Gratitude is about making honest contact with what has been given — and choosing to acknowledge it.

I'd like you to take a moment and bring to mind 3 things you are genuinely — honestly — grateful for. Not things you think you should be grateful for. Real things.

[Allow 2–3 minutes for reflection]

Now for each one: what does this gratitude tell you about what you value? About what matters to you?

Gratitude is a form of values recognition — it shows us what we care about by revealing what we notice as a gift.

Is there one person, experience, or quality that you want to explicitly acknowledge or appreciate today? Not to perform gratitude — but to make genuine contact with what is good?"

## Practice 8: Legacy and Contribution Values Work

**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client is seeking a sense of purpose and contribution; working toward finding direction after loss or transition

### Instructions

"Legacy is about what we leave behind — not just material inheritance, but the mark we make on the people and places we touch.

I'd like to invite you to reflect on what you want your legacy to be. Not what you feel pressured to accomplish — but what you genuinely hope to contribute.

Think across several domains:
- What kind of person do you want to have been for the people who know you best?
- What contribution do you want to make to your community or field?
- What values do you want to have demonstrated through your actions?
- What do you want future generations to know or have because of you?

[Allow 5–10 minutes for reflection or writing]

Now: looking at your current life — your daily choices, your use of time, your relationships — how aligned is your current direction with this legacy?

What one change in the coming week would move you toward it?"

## Interfaith and Secular Adaptations

| Practice | Religious Adaptation | Secular Adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| Values Excavation | Frame as discernment prayer; use scripture references where meaningful | Frame as clarifying what you stand for; use philosophical language |
| Acceptance of Uncertainty | Connect to theological concepts of mystery, faith, unknowing | Connect to philosophical tradition (Socratic not-knowing, Buddhist impermanence) |
| Present-Moment Awareness | Connect to contemplative prayer, mindful presence before God | Connect to mindfulness literature; Kabat-Zinn, Thich Nhat Hanh (secular) |
| Larger Context Exercise | Connect to perspective from God's view; cosmic significance | Connect to scientific cosmology; Carl Sagan-style wonder at scale |
| Legacy Work | Connect to tradition, calling, stewardship | Connect to humanistic contribution; generativity theory (Erikson) |
