# Agentic-Generative Dimension: ABA Protocols

> *"You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems."*
> — James Clear, *Atomic Habits* (adapted)

---

## Overview

This document presents Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) protocols specifically adapted for the Agentic-Generative dimension of the Integrated Adaptive Resilience Framework (IATLAS). These evidence-based, data-driven protocols are designed to systematically build the behavioral repertoire associated with agency, goal pursuit, initiative, self-efficacy, and purposeful action.

ABA's core strengths — precision, measurability, individualization, and demonstrated effectiveness in skill acquisition — make it an ideal foundation for agentic skill development. The protocols in this document are grounded in the science of behavior change and adapted for use in trauma-informed, values-aligned resilience practice.

All protocols should be implemented within the context of a comprehensive functional assessment of the client's current behavioral repertoire, reinforcement history, and barriers to agentic functioning.

---

## Core ABA Principles Applied in the Agentic-Generative Dimension

- **Positive reinforcement**: Systematically reinforce goal-directed behaviors, initiative, and persistence
- **Stimulus control**: Arrange antecedent conditions to support action initiation and values-aligned behavior
- **Shaping**: Reinforce successive approximations toward complex agentic skills
- **Chaining**: Break complex goal-pursuit behaviors into teachable steps
- **Generalization programming**: Deliberately plan for skill transfer across settings and contexts
- **Data collection**: Monitor progress with objective, observable measures to guide clinical decisions
- **Functional assessment**: Understand the variables maintaining avoidance and passivity before introducing change

## Protocol 1: Behavioral Activation

### Purpose
To increase engagement in values-consistent activities, reduce behavioral avoidance, and establish a repertoire of purposeful action, particularly in clients who present with low motivation, depression, or trauma-related withdrawal.

### Theoretical Basis
Behavioral activation draws on Lewinsohn's (1974) activity-mood hypothesis and Martell et al.'s (2001, 2013) behavioral model of depression. Low rates of response-contingent positive reinforcement from the environment maintain avoidance and low mood. Increasing engagement with meaningful activities restores contact with natural reinforcers and builds a positive behavioral history. [Research citation needed]

### Procedure Steps

**Step 1: Activity Monitoring (Week 1)**
- Provide the client with an Activity Log (see measurement.md)
- Instruct the client to record all activities across 7 days, noting time, activity, mood rating (0–10), and sense of accomplishment or pleasure (0–10)
- Review data collaboratively at next session

**Step 2: Functional Analysis of Current Activity Patterns (Session 2)**
- Analyze Activity Log data to identify:
  - Current activity level (high vs. low)
  - Activities that produce positive affect or sense of accomplishment
  - Avoidance patterns and their functional consequences
  - Values-consistent vs. values-inconsistent activity distribution

**Step 3: Values Clarification (Session 2–3)**
- Use values exercises (see values-exercises.md) to identify 3–5 meaningful life domains
- For each domain, identify specific, concrete activities that express those values
- Distinguish between activities that feel meaningful vs. activities that merely feel pleasurable or easy

**Step 4: Activity Hierarchy Construction (Session 3)**
- Collaboratively construct an Activity Hierarchy: a graded list of values-consistent activities ranked by difficulty/avoidance from least to most challenging
- Aim for 10–15 activities spanning easy, moderate, and challenging levels
- Include both solitary and social activities

**Step 5: Behavioral Prescription (Session 3–4)**
- Schedule specific activation activities into the coming week
- Begin with activities at the lower end of the hierarchy
- Use implementation intentions: "When [situation], I will [behavior] at [time/place]"
- Establish a clear, manageable schedule — not overwhelming

**Step 6: Completion Monitoring and Reinforcement (Ongoing)**
- Review scheduled activities at each session
- Reinforce completion of scheduled activities verbally and through recognition of progress
- Use data to demonstrate the connection between activation and wellbeing outcomes
- Address barriers that prevented activation using problem-solving

**Step 7: Progressive Challenge (Ongoing)**
- Gradually increase the challenge level of scheduled activities as easier activities are established
- Use data from mood/accomplishment ratings to guide progression
- Introduce activities from middle and upper ranges of Activity Hierarchy

**Step 8: Natural Reinforcement Transfer**
- Monitor for shift from clinician-delivered reinforcement to naturally occurring reinforcement (sense of accomplishment, social connection, etc.)
- Plan for maintenance of activation gains when clinical contact reduces

### Data Collection
- Activity Log: daily record of activities, duration, mood (0–10), accomplishment (0–10)
- Session review: number of scheduled activities completed vs. planned
- Weekly Activation Score: sum of accomplishment ratings across week

### Expected Outcomes
- Increased weekly activity count within 4–6 weeks
- Improved mood ratings following activated activities (vs. non-activated days)
- Reduced avoidance behavior frequency
- Client reports sense of connection between action and wellbeing

### Troubleshooting
- *Client does not complete scheduled activities*: Review schedule for feasibility; assess for barriers; reduce schedule difficulty; increase prompting strategies
- *Activities produce no pleasure or accomplishment*: Reassess values alignment; ensure activities are personally meaningful, not clinician-selected; assess for anhedonia requiring additional clinical consideration
- *Client avoids Activity Log*: Simplify recording; try phone-based tracking; address avoidance as a target behavior itself

## Protocol 2: Task Analysis for Goal Pursuit

### Purpose
To break complex goal-pursuit behaviors into discrete, teachable steps that can be shaped, practiced, and reinforced systematically.

### Task Analysis: Goal-Setting Behavior

| Step | Behavior | Mastery Criterion |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Identify a life domain important to the client | Names a domain without prompting; 3 consecutive sessions |
| 2 | Articulate a value in that domain | States a value in behavioral terms (what they want to do/be, not just feel) |
| 3 | Generate an outcome goal in that domain | States a specific, observable outcome goal; not vague wish |
| 4 | Break outcome goal into 3+ process goals | Lists process goals that are within their control and behavioral |
| 5 | Identify first action step for nearest process goal | Specifies action, time, and place; 80% accuracy over 3 sessions |
| 6 | Identify potential barriers | Names at least 2 internal and 1 external barrier |
| 7 | Develop barrier response plans | States a specific "if barrier, then action" plan for each barrier |
| 8 | Schedule action step | Places action step in calendar/schedule |
| 9 | Complete action step | Reports completion with verifiable detail |
| 10 | Review and adjust | Reviews outcomes; adjusts next steps based on what was learned |

### Implementation Notes
- Teach each step explicitly; do not assume clients have this repertoire
- Use modeling, role play, and written practice alongside verbal instruction
- Assess which steps are already in the client's repertoire and begin shaping from that point
- Reinforce demonstration of each step before introducing the next

## Protocol 3: Shaping Protocols for Self-Efficacy

### Purpose
To build a functional sense of confidence in action-taking through systematic reinforcement of increasingly challenging approaches to self-efficacy-related behaviors.

### Shaping Sequence

**Baseline**: Client expresses significant self-doubt; avoids challenging tasks; relies heavily on clinician reassurance

**Step 1** (Reinforce): Any attempt at self-reference when discussing past successes — even tentative (e.g., "I did manage that once, I think...")

**Step 2** (Reinforce): Specific recall of past successful behaviors relevant to current challenge (e.g., "I handled the problem at work last month...")

**Step 3** (Reinforce): Connecting past success to current challenge (e.g., "If I could do that, maybe I could do this...")

**Step 4** (Reinforce): Generation of an efficacy statement without prompting (e.g., "I think I can try this...")

**Step 5** (Reinforce): Action commitment based on efficacy statement (e.g., "I'll try it this week")

**Step 6** (Reinforce): Completion of attempted behavior and verbal account of competence

**Step 7** (Reinforce): Spontaneous, unprompted efficacy statements prior to challenging tasks

**Step 8** (Maintain): Consistent, independent use of efficacy statements as part of natural behavioral repertoire

### Clinical Notes
- Use verbal reinforcement immediately following each approximation
- Keep reinforcement natural and descriptive (e.g., "You just connected your past success to this challenge — that's exactly the kind of thinking that builds confidence")
- Do not jump past shaping steps — premature challenge will extinguish the behavior
- If shaping stalls, return to previous step and ensure reinforcement is effective

## Reinforcement Schedules

| Target Behavior | Recommended Schedule | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Initial action initiation | Continuous (FR1) | Build behavior strength early; reinforce every attempt |
| Goal completion (early) | FR1 → FR2 → FR3 | Gradual thinning to build persistence |
| Persistence through obstacles | Variable ratio (VR2–VR3) | Produces more durable, resistant behavior |
| Self-monitoring records | Fixed interval (FI weekly) | Predictable schedule supports routine formation |
| Advanced skills (mentor, teach) | Natural/intermittent | Mirror natural environment reinforcement |
| Values-aligned daily actions | Variable interval (VI daily) | Naturalistic; prevent schedule dependence |

### Notes on Reinforcement
- Assess individual reinforcers: what functions as a reinforcer varies across clients
- Include social reinforcement, activity reinforcement, and internal reinforcers (sense of accomplishment)
- Plan for natural reinforcer contact — clinical reinforcement should be a bridge, not the destination
- Avoid token economies that feel patronizing for adult or adolescent clients; use functionally meaningful reinforcers

## Protocol 4: Behavioral Chain Interruption and Recovery

When behavioral chains for goal pursuit are interrupted by barriers (avoidance, distraction, emotional dysregulation), practitioners can use chain interruption and re-entry techniques.

### Procedure
1. Identify the specific point in the goal-pursuit chain where interruption occurred
2. Assess the function of the interruption (avoidance, competing behavior, skill deficit, environmental barrier)
3. Restore the discriminative stimulus for goal pursuit (cue the behavior chain from the interrupted step)
4. Use prompting if necessary to re-initiate the chain from the interrupted point
5. Reinforce re-entry and completion
6. Debrief the interruption point: what can be modified to prevent future interruption?

## Protocol 5: Antecedent Modification Strategies

Antecedent modification reduces barriers to action initiation by arranging the environment to support goal-directed behavior.

**Key antecedent modifications for agentic behavior:**

| Antecedent Modification | Target Behavior | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Implementation intentions | Action initiation | "When I sit down after dinner, I will work on my resume for 20 minutes" |
| Environmental arrangement | Task engagement | Set out materials the night before; remove competing stimuli |
| Visual prompts | Goal salience | Post goal card in visible location; phone reminders |
| Stimulus narrowing | Focus and concentration | Designated workspace; phone in another room |
| Habit stacking | Routine formation | Add new behavior after established routine trigger |
| Reducing response effort | Initiation of difficult tasks | Break first step into smallest possible unit |

## Protocol 6: Generalization and Maintenance Planning

ABA protocols are only clinically valuable if skills generalize beyond the training context. Generalization planning must be built into every protocol from the outset.

### Generalization Strategies
- **Train in multiple contexts**: Practice goal-setting, activation, and problem-solving in varied settings
- **Use natural reinforcers**: Pair clinical reinforcement with naturally occurring reinforcers from the start
- **Vary trainers**: If possible, involve multiple people who can support skill generalization
- **Use common stimuli**: Ensure that environmental triggers for skill use in the real world are included in training
- **Program for maintenance**: Schedule check-ins and maintenance sessions

### Maintenance Schedule
- Weekly sessions during active skill-building
- Bi-weekly sessions during consolidation phase
- Monthly check-ins during maintenance phase
- PRN (as needed) contact for relapse prevention and skill refreshing

## Special Considerations

### Trauma-Informed Modifications
- Never use punitive consequences for non-completion; use curiosity and problem-solving instead
- Recognize that action initiation deficits may be trauma responses (shutdown, freeze, dissociation)
- Ensure physical and relational safety before introducing behavioral challenge
- Move at client pace; avoid overcorrection when progress is slow

### Cultural Adaptations
- Collaboratively define what "agency" means within the client's cultural context
- Include collective goals alongside individual goals where culturally appropriate
- Adapt Activity Hierarchies to include culturally meaningful activities
- Adjust reinforcement approaches to be culturally congruent

## Integration with ACT

ABA protocols for the Agentic-Generative dimension work best when integrated with ACT's values clarification, defusion, and committed action framework:

- **Values work** (ACT) provides direction; **behavioral planning** (ABA) provides structure
- **Defusion from self-doubt** (ACT) removes cognitive barriers; **shaping for self-efficacy** (ABA) builds behavioral confidence
- **Acceptance of discomfort** (ACT) supports persistence; **reinforcement of persistence** (ABA) strengthens the behavior
- **Present-moment awareness** (ACT) supports contact with current reinforcers; **activity scheduling** (ABA) ensures those contacts occur
