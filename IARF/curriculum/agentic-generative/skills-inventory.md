# Agentic-Generative Dimension: Skills Inventory

> *"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom."*
> — Viktor Frankl (attributed)

---

## Overview

The Agentic-Generative dimension of the IATLAS addresses the individual's capacity to initiate purposeful action, pursue meaningful goals, navigate obstacles, and sustain behavioral momentum in the direction of their values. Rooted in both Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) and Acceptance & Commitment Therapy (ACT), this dimension recognizes that resilience is not merely the absence of distress — it is the active expression of agency in the face of adversity.

"Agentic" refers to the capacity for self-directed, intentional action. "Generative" refers to the capacity to produce, create, and contribute — to generate outcomes, relationships, and meaning rather than merely survive. Together, these qualities form the behavioral spine of resilience: the ability to move toward what matters even when circumstances are difficult.

This skills inventory provides a structured, evidence-based map of the competencies associated with agentic-generative functioning. It is organized into three developmental levels — Foundational, Intermediate, and Advanced — to support individualized, data-driven, values-aligned skill development within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

---

## Core Skill Domains

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **Goal-Setting** | The capacity to identify, articulate, and pursue personally meaningful goals with clarity and direction |
| **Problem-Solving** | The behavioral repertoire for identifying obstacles, generating solutions, and implementing adaptive responses |
| **Self-Efficacy** | The belief in one's capacity to execute behavior required to achieve outcomes; functional confidence in action |
| **Initiative** | The ability to begin action independently, without external prompting; self-starting under conditions of uncertainty |
| **Purposeful Action** | Behavior that is consistently oriented toward values-based goals, even in the presence of difficult thoughts, emotions, or circumstances |

## Skills Inventory

### Level 1: Foundational Skills

*These skills form the essential building blocks of agentic functioning. They are prioritized in early intervention and with clients who have limited prior experience with goal-directed behavior or who have experienced significant learned helplessness.*

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Values Identification** | The ability to identify and articulate what matters most in one's life across key domains | Can name at least 3 personal values; can distinguish values from goals; can describe how current behavior does or does not align with values |
| **Basic Goal Setting** | The ability to translate a value or desire into a concrete, achievable behavioral goal | Can state a goal in specific, observable terms; distinguishes between short-term and long-term goals; can identify at least one first step toward a goal |
| **Action Initiation** | The ability to begin a task or behavior without excessive delay or prompting from others | Demonstrates spontaneous initiation of tasks; begins work within a reasonable latency period; does not require repeated external prompting to start |
| **Simple Problem Identification** | The ability to recognize when a goal is blocked and name the specific obstacle | Can articulate the barrier (internal or external) preventing goal progress; distinguishes between solvable and unsolvable obstacles |
| **Help Seeking** | The ability to identify when support is needed and request it appropriately | Asks for help from appropriate sources; can specify the type of help needed; initiates help-seeking before crisis rather than only during crisis |
| **Task Completion** | The ability to follow through on a begun task to a satisfactory endpoint | Completes tasks within agreed timeframes; demonstrates follow-through across multiple sessions; does not abandon tasks at point of difficulty without problem-solving attempt |
| **Positive Self-Talk** | The use of self-encouraging language that supports continued action | Can generate at least one encouraging statement when facing difficulty; uses self-talk to reduce avoidance behaviors; demonstrates awareness of the influence of internal language on behavior |
| **Behavioral Activation** | Engagement in meaningful activities even in the presence of low motivation or negative affect | Participates in scheduled activities regardless of mood state; reports increased sense of meaning or accomplishment following activation; demonstrates reduction in avoidance behaviors over time |

---

### Level 2: Intermediate Skills

*These skills extend foundational competencies into more complex, autonomous, and flexible behavioral patterns. They are introduced once Level 1 skills are demonstrated consistently across at least two contexts.*

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Multi-Step Goal Planning** | The ability to construct a sequential plan for achieving a goal that involves multiple interdependent steps | Can create a written goal plan with at least 3 steps; identifies dependencies between steps; revises plan when circumstances change |
| **Barrier Identification** | Systematic identification of both internal (cognitive, emotional) and external (situational, relational) barriers to goal pursuit | Can categorize barriers as internal vs. external; identifies specific barriers before they derail action; uses barrier identification as a proactive rather than reactive process |
| **Flexible Problem Solving** | The ability to generate multiple potential solutions to an obstacle and select among them adaptively | Generates at least 3 candidate solutions for a given problem; weighs pros and cons; does not rigidly persist with a failed strategy |
| **Self-Monitoring** | Systematic observation and recording of one's own behavior, progress, and outcomes | Keeps behavioral records or journals; can identify patterns in their own behavior over time; uses self-monitoring data to adjust behavior |
| **Initiative Under Uncertainty** | Taking action toward goals even when the outcome is unclear or the path is ambiguous | Demonstrates action-taking in novel situations; tolerates not knowing and acts anyway; reflects on outcomes to guide future action |
| **Persistence Through Obstacles** | The ability to maintain goal-directed behavior in the face of setbacks, frustration, or extended difficulty | Returns to goal pursuit after setback; shows reduced time-to-recovery from obstacles over time; demonstrates stable goal engagement across sessions |
| **Self-Efficacy Statements** | The ability to construct and use specific, evidence-based efficacy statements that support action | Can identify past successes relevant to current challenge; generates specific (not generic) statements of confidence; efficacy statements are linked to observable behavioral evidence |
| **Values-Action Alignment** | Actively monitoring and adjusting behavior to ensure consistency with stated values | Can identify discrepancies between values and behavior; takes corrective action when misalignment is identified; regularly uses values as a decision-making guide |

---

### Level 3: Advanced Skills

*These skills represent the highest expression of agentic-generative functioning. They are associated with self-directed resilience, leadership, and the capacity to support others in agentic development. They typically emerge after sustained work across Levels 1 and 2.*

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Long-Range Vision Planning** | The capacity to identify and pursue goals with 1–5 year horizons, maintaining direction over extended time periods | Can articulate a vision for their life 3–5 years forward; maintains behavioral momentum toward long-range goals; adjusts tactics while maintaining strategic vision |
| **Complex Problem Navigation** | The ability to navigate multi-layered problems involving multiple stakeholders, competing demands, and uncertain outcomes | Demonstrates systematic approach to complex problems; effectively coordinates problem-solving across domains; manages partial solutions and iterative progress |
| **Self-Directed Behavior Change** | The ability to independently identify target behaviors, implement change strategies, and evaluate outcomes without clinician prompting | Designs their own behavior change plans; implements self-reinforcement and self-monitoring; evaluates plan effectiveness and revises independently |
| **Mentorship and Leadership** | The capacity to guide, support, and inspire others in agentic skill development | Demonstrates patience and evidence-based encouragement with others; models values-aligned behavior; actively contributes to community or group resilience |
| **Purposeful Risk-Taking** | Willingness to engage in uncertain, effortful, or vulnerable action in service of values | Takes calculated risks consistent with values; distinguishes purposeful risk from reckless action; recovers effectively from negative risk outcomes |
| **Goal Revision** | The sophisticated ability to revise, reframe, or release goals when contextual changes demand adaptive flexibility | Can let go of goals that are no longer values-consistent; reframes setbacks as information rather than failure; demonstrates flexible persistence vs. rigid persistence |
| **Teaching Goal-Setting** | The capacity to effectively teach goal-setting skills to others | Can explain goal-setting principles clearly; adapts teaching to learner's level; models and coaches the skill with others; provides effective feedback |
| **Resilient Agency** | Sustained agentic functioning over extended periods under conditions of ongoing adversity | Demonstrates consistent values-aligned action over 6+ months; shows no significant regression under stress; effectively uses all Level 1–3 skills fluidly and contextually |

## Skill Prerequisites

The following prerequisites are recommended for structured skill-building. Practitioners should assess current level before selecting skill targets.

- **Basic Goal Setting** → prerequisite for Multi-Step Goal Planning
- **Values Identification** → prerequisite for Values-Action Alignment and Purposeful Action
- **Action Initiation** → prerequisite for Initiative Under Uncertainty and Behavioral Activation
- **Simple Problem Identification** → prerequisite for Flexible Problem Solving and Barrier Identification
- **Self-Monitoring** → prerequisite for Self-Directed Behavior Change
- **Help Seeking** → prerequisite for Mentorship and Leadership (transition from help-seeker to helper)
- **Persistence Through Obstacles** → prerequisite for Resilient Agency
- **Values-Action Alignment** → prerequisite for Long-Range Vision Planning

[Clinical example: A client who demonstrates strong Basic Goal Setting but struggles with Barrier Identification may benefit from structured antecedent mapping before progressing to Multi-Step Goal Planning]

## Cross-Dimensional Connections

The Agentic-Generative dimension does not operate in isolation. Agentic skills are deeply interconnected with all five other IATLAS dimensions:

| Connected Dimension | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Relational-Connective** | Help-seeking and initiative are social behaviors requiring relational skills; mentorship is both agentic and relational |
| **Spiritual-Reflective** | Values identification is foundational to both dimensions; long-range vision requires meaning and purpose |
| **Emotional-Adaptive** | Behavioral activation requires emotional tolerance; persistence through obstacles involves emotion regulation; psychological flexibility bridges both dimensions |
| **Somatic-Regulative** | Physical energy and somatic regulation support initiative and goal pursuit; fatigue and somatic distress are barriers to action |
| **Cognitive-Narrative** | Self-efficacy statements are cognitive; problem identification and flexible problem solving are cognitive-behavioral; narrative of agency supports agentic behavior |

## Assessment & Baseline Considerations

Before introducing Agentic-Generative skills work, practitioners should conduct a baseline assessment that includes:

1. **Behavioral history**: What does the client's behavioral repertoire currently look like? What goal-directed behaviors are already present?
2. **Avoidance patterns**: What behaviors are the client avoiding? What are the functional consequences maintaining avoidance?
3. **Values clarity**: Can the client identify meaningful values to provide direction for goal-setting?
4. **Reinforcement history**: What has been reinforcing for this client? What has been aversive?
5. **Self-efficacy**: What does the client believe about their capacity to change and grow?
6. **Trauma history**: How has past adversity affected the client's experience of agency and control?

**Recommended baseline assessment tools:**
- Valued Living Questionnaire (VLQ; Wilson et al.) [Research citation needed]
- General Self-Efficacy Scale (Schwarzer & Jerusalem, 1995) [Research citation needed]

## Notes for Clinicians

### Working with Learned Helplessness

Clients who have experienced prolonged adversity, trauma, or failure may present with significant learned helplessness — a generalized expectation that action will not produce desired outcomes. For these clients:
- Begin with very small, highly achievable action steps to build a reinforcement history
- Use shaping aggressively: reinforce approximations
- Emphasize the client's existing successes and competencies
- Use defusion from hopelessness thoughts rather than cognitive challenging
- Be patient — building agency after learned helplessness takes time

### Cultural Considerations

The concept of individual agency varies significantly across cultures. In collectivistic contexts, agency may be experienced as embedded in community action rather than individual action. Practitioners should:
- Explore clients' cultural frameworks for understanding initiative and action
- Adapt goal-setting language to reflect collective as well as individual goals
- Recognize that help-seeking in some cultural contexts is a sign of strength, not weakness
- Avoid imposing individualistic frameworks on clients who organize their agency within community structures

### Trauma-Informed Practice

Trauma can significantly disrupt agentic functioning through dissociation, hypervigilance, shame, and disrupted sense of self. Practitioners should:
- Proceed at the client's pace; do not push for action before safety and stabilization
- Normalize difficulty with action initiation as a trauma response, not a character deficit
- Connect behavioral activation gently to the body (somatic-regulative link)
- Use ACT defusion techniques to address shame and self-doubt that block action
