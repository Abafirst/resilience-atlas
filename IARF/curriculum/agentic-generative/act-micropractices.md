# Agentic-Generative Dimension: ACT Micropractices

> *"Committed action is not about being perfect. It's about being consistent in the direction of what matters, even when it's hard."*
> — Adapted from Hayes, Strosahl & Wilson

---

## Overview

Acceptance & Commitment Therapy (ACT) offers a powerful set of practices that directly enhance agentic-generative functioning. Where ABA protocols build the behavioral structure of goal pursuit, ACT micropractices build the psychological foundation — the inner spaciousness, values clarity, and committed action orientation that make purposeful behavior sustainable under adversity.

This document presents eight core ACT micropractices specifically adapted for the Agentic-Generative dimension. Each practice targets one or more processes from the ACT hexaflex — acceptance, defusion, present-moment awareness, self-as-context, values, and committed action — as they relate to agency, initiative, and purposeful living within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

Practitioners are encouraged to integrate these practices fluidly with the ABA protocols in aba-protocols.md, using clinical judgment to determine which practice to introduce based on the client's current functional needs.

---

## ACT Hexaflex in the Agentic Context

| ACT Process | Agentic-Generative Application |
|---|---|
| **Acceptance** | Willingness to feel fear, doubt, or discomfort without letting these prevent action |
| **Defusion** | Unhooking from "I can't" thoughts, self-doubt, and catastrophic predictions that block initiative |
| **Present-Moment Awareness** | Engaging fully with the current action step rather than ruminating on past failures or worrying about future outcomes |
| **Self-as-Context** | Distinguishing the observing self from the story of limitation and incompetence |
| **Values** | Connecting action to what matters, not just what feels safe or comfortable |
| **Committed Action** | Building patterns of purposeful behavior that persist through difficulty and setback |

## Practice 1: Values Excavation

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Client has difficulty identifying what they genuinely care about; goals feel arbitrary or externally imposed

### Instructions

*Say to client:*

"I'd like to try an exercise that helps us dig down to what genuinely matters to you — not what you think should matter, or what others expect of you, but what's real for you at a deep level. There's no right or wrong answer here.

Close your eyes if that feels okay, or soften your gaze. Take a breath or two and let your body settle.

Now I'd like you to call to mind a moment — it could be recent or from years ago — when you felt truly alive. A moment when you were doing something that mattered, even if it was difficult. When time seemed to organize itself around what you were doing. Take a moment to let that image form...

[Pause 30 seconds]

Stay with that memory. Where are you? Who else, if anyone, is there? What are you doing? What does it feel like in your body?

[Pause 30 seconds]

Now, keeping that memory present, I'd like you to notice: what does this moment tell you about what you care about? What value or quality of living is this moment an expression of?

[Pause 20 seconds]

Gently open your eyes when you're ready."

### Debrief Questions
- "What value or values came up for you in that memory?"
- "How present is that value in your current life? In your current goals?"
- "If you lived more fully from that value this week, what would change about your behavior?"
- "What gets in the way of living from this value?"

### Clinical Notes
- If the client cannot find a memory of feeling alive, use an alternative prompt: "A time when you were angry about something — what does that anger tell you about what you care about?"
- Some clients will confuse values (directions of action) with goals (specific outcomes). Gently clarify the distinction.
- [Clinical example: A client who identified "caring for others" as a core value through this exercise began scheduling weekly calls to family members — a behavioral activation target that felt impossible until it was connected to this value.]

## Practice 2: The Compass Activity

**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client is unclear about direction; all goals feel equally important or unimportant; client lacks a sense of where to go next

### Instructions

Provide the client with a Compass worksheet — a simple visual of a compass with four quadrants labeled: Work/Career, Relationships, Health/Body, and Personal Growth (adapt quadrants to client's context).

"Think of values like a compass rather than a destination. A compass doesn't tell you where you are — it tells you which direction to face. Even when the path is rough or the weather is bad, the compass gives you direction.

In each section of this compass, I'd like you to write or say what matters to you in that area of life — not what you've achieved, but what you want to move toward. What kind of person do you want to be in each area?

[Allow time for client to work]

Now, looking at your compass: if you had to choose one direction to move toward this week — just one step — where would it be? What would that step look like in terms of actual behavior?"

## Practice 3: Committed Action Planning

**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Committed Action, Values, Acceptance
**Best Used When**: Client identifies values but struggles to translate them into action; "I know what matters but I don't do anything about it"

### Instructions

"Let's take one value you've identified — something that genuinely matters to you — and build a committed action plan around it. A committed action is different from a good intention: it's a specific behavior, at a specific time, in a specific place, that you're willing to do regardless of how you feel at the time.

Let's start by identifying the value: ___________________

Now, what behavior would express that value? Let's get very specific.

Behavior: ___________________
When: ___________________
Where: ___________________
For how long/how many times: ___________________

Now here's the important part. What's going to show up — internally — that might try to pull you off course? Thoughts, feelings, urges? Let's name them now, before they arrive.

[List anticipated internal barriers]

And knowing those things might show up... are you willing to have them while doing the behavior anyway?

[Explore willingness; use acceptance exercises if needed]

This is what committed action looks like in ACT: it's not about having no doubt or fear. It's about being willing to carry those things with you while you walk in your chosen direction."

## Practice 4: The Passenger Bus Metaphor

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion, Acceptance, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client describes being "hijacked" by self-doubt, fear, or self-critical thoughts that stop action

### Full Script

"I'd like to share a metaphor that might be useful for what you're describing — this experience of having thoughts and feelings that seem to take over and stop you from doing what matters.

Imagine you're driving a bus. You're in the driver's seat, and the bus is headed in a direction you've chosen — toward your values, toward what matters. You know where you're going.

But the bus has passengers. Passengers you didn't necessarily choose. There's the self-doubt passenger — the one who says 'you're not good enough for this.' There's the fear passenger. Maybe the shame passenger. Maybe the exhaustion passenger. They're all on the bus with you.

Here's what often happens: the passengers get loud, or they come to the front, and we make a deal with them. We say 'okay, I'll stop driving if you just quiet down.' And we pull over. And the bus stops. And maybe they quiet down for a moment. But now we're not moving, and the passengers are still there.

The alternative — and this is the ACT idea — is to let the passengers be on the bus while you keep driving. You don't have to argue with them, reason with them, or prove them wrong. You don't have to throw them off the bus. You just... drive. They can say what they want. You know where you're going.

What passengers are on your bus right now? And what would it mean to keep driving with them there?"

### Debrief Questions
- "Which passenger shows up most often when you try to take action?"
- "What 'deal' do you usually make with that passenger?"
- "What would it look like to keep driving with that passenger present?"

## Practice 5: Willingness and Action

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Acceptance, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client avoids action because of anticipated discomfort or fear of failure

### Instructions

"There's a distinction in ACT between willingness and wanting. Willingness isn't about wanting to feel uncomfortable or wanting to fail. It's about being open to whatever shows up while doing what matters.

Let me ask you: on a scale from 0–10, how willing are you — right now — to experience [name specific feared experience] while taking [specific action]?

[Explore the rating. If low:]

What would need to be different for that number to go up a point? Not ten points — just one.

[Explore. Then:]

What if willingness isn't something you have to build up first before acting — what if willingness is the act itself? That taking the action *is* the willingness.

What's one small action you could take today that would be an act of willingness?"

## Practice 6: Defusion for Self-Doubt

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion
**Best Used When**: Self-doubt thoughts are creating significant avoidance of valued action

### Specific Defusion Techniques for "I Can't" Thoughts

**Technique 1: Labeling**
"Notice the thought 'I can't do this' — and add 'I'm having the thought that I can't do this.' Does adding those words change anything?"

**Technique 2: Thanking the Mind**
"Your mind is trying to protect you. When it says 'you can't do this,' it's trying to keep you safe from failure or embarrassment. Thank it. 'Thanks, mind. I see you're trying to help.' And then... what's the next step toward your value?"

**Technique 3: Ancient Radio**
"Imagine that thought — 'I'm not capable' — coming from an old, slightly fuzzy radio playing in the corner of the room. The radio is on. The song is playing. You can hear it. But you don't have to dance to it."

**Technique 4: Writing the Thought Down**
"Write the thought down on paper. Look at it. It's just words now — ink on a page. The same words that have been running your life. How different does it look from this angle?"

## Practice 7: Values-Based Behavioral Activation

**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Committed Action, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Integrating ACT and ABA approaches; connecting behavioral activation directly to values

### Instructions

"In behavioral activation, we schedule activities that increase your contact with meaningful experience. In ACT, we anchor those activities to your values. Together, they're very powerful.

Let's identify one area of your life where you've been less active or less engaged than you'd like to be.

[Identify area]

What value is connected to that area for you?

[Identify value]

What's one specific, concrete activity in the next 7 days that would express that value? Something you're willing to try even if your mood is low or the thought 'what's the point' shows up?

[Identify activity, time, place — implementation intention format]

Now: what internal experience might show up that could get in the way? And are you willing to have that experience while doing this activity anyway?

[Explore willingness; confirm commitment]

Let's write this down as a commitment — not a hope, but a commitment."

## Practice 8: Present-Moment Action

**Duration**: 5–10 minutes (can be used as a session opener)
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client is caught in rumination about past failures or worry about future outcomes, preventing engagement with current action steps

### Instructions

"Before we dive in today, I'd like to take a moment to arrive here fully. Close your eyes or soften your gaze.

Take three slow breaths. With each breath, let go of where you were before this session — the commute, the morning, yesterday.

Now, with your eyes still closed: what's one thing you want to bring to this session today? Not a problem to solve — a direction to move toward. A value to act from.

[Pause 20 seconds]

Open your eyes. What did you notice?"

## Group Adaptation Notes

All practices in this document can be adapted for group delivery:
- **Values Excavation**: Use dyad sharing followed by group debrief
- **Compass Activity**: Use small groups to share and compare compasses; identify shared values
- **Passenger Bus**: Role-play as a group — assign participants as different passengers; facilitator "drives"
- **Defusion techniques**: Practice in pairs; one person states a self-doubt thought, partner practices defusion technique in response
