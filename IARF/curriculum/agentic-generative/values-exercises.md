# Agentic-Generative Dimension: Values Exercises

> *"He who has a why to live for can bear almost any how."*
> — Friedrich Nietzsche (attributed)

---

## Overview

Values work is the foundation of the Agentic-Generative dimension. Without a clear sense of direction — of what matters and why — behavioral activation is hollow, goal-setting is arbitrary, and persistence in the face of adversity is fragile. The exercises in this document are designed to help clients excavate, clarify, and act from their deepest values within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

These exercises draw from the ACT tradition (Hayes, Wilson, Luoma), meaning-centered psychotherapy (Frankl, Wong), and narrative approaches to identity and purpose. They are designed to be clinically rich and practically actionable — connecting values clarity directly to the purposeful behavioral choices that constitute resilience.

Values, in the IATLAS framework, are not moral rules or obligations. They are chosen directions — qualities of living that give action its meaning. Values are not destinations (goals) but orientations that can be expressed in any moment regardless of circumstances.

## Exercise 1: The Life Areas Values Sort

**Purpose**: To systematically identify values across key life domains
**Duration**: 30–45 minutes
**Modality**: Individual or group (small group debrief version available)

### Instructions

"This exercise helps you identify what genuinely matters to you across the main areas of your life. There's no right or wrong — this is about discovering what's true for you, not what you think you should value.

Read through the life areas below. For each one, reflect briefly on: What qualities of how I live this area of my life matter most to me? What kind of person do I want to be here?"

### Life Areas Values Table

| Life Area | What Matters to Me Here | Current Alignment (1–10) | Desired Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Family and close relationships | | | |
| Friendships and social life | | | |
| Romantic partnership | | | |
| Work, career, or vocation | | | |
| Education and personal growth | | | |
| Health and physical wellbeing | | | |
| Leisure, creativity, and play | | | |
| Community and contribution | | | |
| Spirituality or meaning | | | |
| Financial wellbeing | | | |

"Now look across your completed table. Which area shows the biggest gap between importance and current alignment? That gap is information — it points to where values-based action could make the most difference."

### Facilitator Notes
- Allow adequate time for reflection; do not rush this exercise
- Help clients distinguish values from outcomes: "healthy relationships" is a value; "getting married" is a goal
- If a client rates everything low in the Current Alignment column, explore whether depression or hopelessness is affecting their self-assessment
- [Clinical example: A client discovered through this exercise that "creativity" rated 9 in importance and 2 in current alignment — leading to a behavioral activation plan incorporating art-making for the first time in years]

## Exercise 2: Obituary / Eulogy Exercise

**Purpose**: To access deep meaning through future-projection; to identify legacy values
**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual (written or verbal); can be adapted for group with care

### Instructions

"This is an exercise that asks you to step into the future — all the way to the end of your life. Some people find this confronting; if you need to slow down at any point, that's fine.

Imagine you've lived a long, full life — a life that, in the ways that mattered most, was well-lived. You've arrived at the end of that life. People who love you have gathered.

Someone who knew you well — someone who saw you at your best — stands up to speak. What do they say about you? Not about your achievements or credentials. About who you were. About how you made people feel. About what you stood for.

Take 10–15 minutes to write (or speak) that eulogy in the first person — as if you are that person delivering it. Let yourself imagine it in full."

### Debrief Questions
- "What values came through in what you wrote?"
- "Looking at that eulogy: how close is the life it describes to the life you're currently living?"
- "What would need to change in the next week, the next year, for that eulogy to be more accurate?"

### Facilitator Notes
- For clients with trauma history or suicidal ideation history, use this exercise carefully and only when the therapeutic relationship is well established
- The exercise can be adapted: "A letter written by your best friend" or "A toast at your 80th birthday" for clients who find the death framing too activating
- Group delivery: use in dyads with reflection sharing; do not require whole-group sharing of eulogies

## Exercise 3: The 80-Year-Old Self Letter

**Purpose**: To access wisdom from a future self; to clarify values for current decision-making
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual or group

### Instructions

"Imagine yourself at age 80 — or at an age that represents the fullness of a life well-lived for you. You've seen a lot. You know what matters. You know what doesn't. You've made mistakes and learned from them. You've experienced both loss and joy.

From that vantage point, your 80-year-old self is going to write a letter to the version of you that exists right now — at your current age, facing your current challenges.

What would that older, wiser self want to say to you? What would they want you to know? What would they encourage you toward? What would they tell you to stop wasting time on?

Take 15 minutes to write this letter."

### Debrief Questions
- "What did your 80-year-old self know that your present self is struggling to remember?"
- "What was the most important piece of wisdom in the letter?"
- "What's one thing this letter suggests you should do — or stop doing — this week?"

## Exercise 4: The Values Thermometer

**Purpose**: To assess current alignment between stated values and daily behavior; to identify specific action targets
**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**Modality**: Individual or group

### Instructions

"For each of the values you've identified as important to you, I'd like you to rate:
1. How important is this value to you? (0 = not at all important, 10 = extremely important)
2. How consistently are you living this value right now? (0 = not at all, 10 = completely)

The 'temperature gap' between these two numbers tells you something important."

"Look at the gaps. A large gap isn't a judgment — it's information. It tells you where your energy wants to go. Which gap is most important to address first?"

### Facilitator Notes
- Normalize large gaps — they are the norm, especially early in treatment
- Use the gaps to collaboratively set behavioral targets for the coming weeks
- Re-administer every 4–6 weeks to track values alignment over time as a progress measure

## Exercise 5: The Two Mountains Metaphor

**Purpose**: To clarify the distinction between values and goals; to prevent goal attachment at the expense of values engagement
**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**Modality**: Psychoeducational; can precede any values work

### Instructions

"Imagine two mountains.

The first mountain is a goal. It's a specific peak. You can see it from here. You know what it looks like when you reach the top. You know when you've arrived and when you haven't. Goals are like this — specific, achievable, and finished when completed.

The second mountain is a value. The second mountain isn't a peak to reach — it's a direction to walk. It doesn't end when you get somewhere. You can always walk a little further in that direction. Even when you reach what you thought was the peak, there's more mountain.

For example: 'running a 5K race' is a goal — first mountain. 'Taking care of my body' is a value — second mountain. You can express 'taking care of my body' on days you don't run, on days you're sick, on days when running is impossible.

Why does this matter? Because if your only reason to act is to reach the first mountain — to achieve the goal — then when the goal is blocked or lost, your reason to act disappears. But if your action is anchored to the second mountain — to the value — then even when goals are disrupted, you can still move in a meaningful direction.

What are your second mountains? What are the values that you can always move toward, regardless of what goals come and go?"

## Exercise 6: Meaningful Moments Inventory

**Purpose**: To connect current values to lived experience; to build a reinforcement history for values-aligned behavior
**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written reflection

### Instructions

"Think back across your life — recent and distant — and identify 5–8 moments when you were doing something that felt genuinely meaningful. Not necessarily pleasant or easy — just meaningful. Moments when you felt, 'This is what I'm for. This is real.'

For each moment, record:"

"Look across your inventory. What themes emerge? What values show up repeatedly? These are your core agentic values — the ones with roots deep enough to sustain purposeful action."

## Exercise 7: Values-Based Weekly Planning

**Purpose**: To translate values clarification into a concrete behavioral plan for the coming week
**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; use as closing exercise or session homework assignment

### Instructions

"Using the values you've identified, let's build a plan for this week that deliberately includes behavior that expresses each of your top values.

For each value, identify one specific, behavioral action you will take this week. Not a hope — a plan. Specific, observable, and scheduled."

## Group vs. Individual Delivery Adaptations

| Exercise | Individual Adaptation | Group Adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| Life Areas Values Sort | Extended reflection; written completion | Small group sharing; identify shared values |
| Obituary Exercise | Written form; deep individual processing | Dyad sharing; no whole-group reading |
| 80-Year-Old Letter | Private; can be kept confidential | Share selected sentences; protect privacy |
| Values Thermometer | Personal tool; track over time | Anonymous aggregate for group discussion |
| Two Mountains | Didactic; then individual reflection | Group discussion of metaphor; collective values mapping |
| Meaningful Moments | Written inventory | Pair sharing; group themes on whiteboard |
| Values Weekly Plan | Personal commitment | Shared accountability pairs in group |

## Cultural Considerations

- **Collective vs. individual values**: In collectivistic cultures, values are often communal rather than individual. Adapt exercises to include "What does my family/community value? What role do I play in those shared values?" alongside individual values.
- **Deference and obligation**: Some clients will list values that are obligations imposed by family or culture rather than chosen values. Gently explore: "Is this something you've chosen to make important, or something you feel you must value?"
- **Spiritual and religious values**: For clients with strong faith traditions, integrate values language with the language of their tradition (calling, stewardship, service, faith). The Spiritual-Reflective dimension exercises are also relevant.
- **Historical trauma**: For clients from communities that have experienced collective trauma (genocide, slavery, forced migration), values work may need to include acknowledgment of intergenerational values transmission and the meaning of survival itself.
