# Emotional-Adaptive Dimension: ABA Protocols

---

## Overview

Applied Behavior Analysis offers systematic, data-driven approaches to building emotional skills and reducing the behavioral consequences of emotional dysregulation. ABA's precise focus on observable behavior makes it uniquely positioned to address the behavioral manifestations of emotional difficulty — including avoidance, emotional outbursts, self-injury, and interpersonal conflict — while the IATLAS's integration with ACT ensures that behavioral change is values-grounded and psychologically flexible.

This document presents ABA protocols specifically adapted for the Emotional-Adaptive dimension of The Resilience Atlas™ IATLAS. All protocols are implemented through a trauma-informed, culturally responsive lens that honors the complexity and dignity of emotional experience.

---

## Protocol 1: Functional Communication Training for Emotional Needs

### Purpose
To replace maladaptive emotional expressions (e.g., tantrums, aggression, withdrawal, self-injury) with functionally equivalent, socially appropriate communicative behaviors that serve the same function.

### Background
FCT is one of the most evidence-based ABA interventions, with robust support across populations and settings. In the emotional context, many challenging behaviors function to communicate unmet emotional needs: "I need validation," "I need space," "I feel overwhelmed," "I need connection." FCT teaches explicit communication as a replacement for these behavioral communicators. [Research citation needed]

### Procedure

**Step 1: Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA)**
- Conduct a comprehensive FBA to identify the function(s) of the target challenging behavior
- Use ABC data, structured interviews, and direct observation
- Identify the specific emotional need the behavior is communicating

**Step 2: Select the Replacement Communication**
- Identify a functionally equivalent communicative behavior that:
  - Serves the same function as the challenging behavior
  - Is contextually appropriate and socially acceptable
  - Is within the client's current communicative repertoire or can be quickly taught

**Step 3: Teach the Replacement**
- Introduce the replacement communication in low-arousal conditions
- Use modeling, prompting, and rehearsal
- Ensure the replacement is immediately and reliably reinforced

**Step 4: Implement and Monitor**
- Use the replacement in naturalistic contexts
- Monitor challenging behavior frequency alongside replacement communication frequency
- Continue to reinforce replacement; do not reinforce challenging behavior

**Example FCT replacements for emotional behaviors:**

| Challenging Behavior | Probable Function | Replacement Communication |
|---|---|---|
| Withdrawal/shutting down | Escape from overwhelming emotion | "I need 5 minutes to settle; I'll be back" |
| Emotional outburst in session | Access validation; escape invalidation | "I feel unheard right now — can you reflect back what you heard?" |
| Excessive reassurance-seeking | Reduce anxiety; access certainty | "I'm feeling anxious and need to hear that you're not leaving" |
| Self-injurious behavior | Regulate extreme distress | Distress tolerance protocol + "I need help right now — I'm overwhelmed" |

## Protocol 2: Emotional Labeling Training

### Purpose
To expand the client's emotional vocabulary through systematic instruction in identifying and naming emotional states.

### Discrete Trial Approach (for limited emotional vocabulary)

**Materials**: Emotion cards (faces, situations, body maps), feeling word list

**Procedure**:
1. Present an emotion card or emotion scenario (SD)
2. Ask: "What emotion is this?" (or "What are they feeling?")
3. Client responds with emotion label
4. Reinforce correct response; provide corrective feedback for errors
5. Begin with high-contrast emotions (happy/sad/afraid/angry) and expand to more nuanced emotions as vocabulary grows

### Naturalistic Approach (for most clients)

**Procedure**:
1. In the natural flow of session, whenever the client displays an emotion, provide a descriptive emotional label: "It sounds like you're feeling [emotion]"
2. Ask the client to confirm, refine, or offer their own label: "Does that fit? Is there another word that's more accurate?"
3. Systematically expand vocabulary over time, introducing nuanced emotion words (apprehensive, melancholic, indignant, sheepish, conflicted)
4. Reinforce use of emotion words with genuine, descriptive acknowledgment

## Protocol 3: Coping Skill Chain Protocol

### Purpose
To teach a behavioral chain for emotion regulation — a sequential set of behaviors that, when performed in order, produces effective emotional regulation.

### The COPE Chain

| Step | Behavior | Description |
|---|---|---|
| C | Check in | Notice what's happening internally: "What am I feeling right now? Where is it in my body?" |
| O | Observe | Observe the emotional state without judgment: "I notice anger. It's tightness in my chest." |
| P | Pause | Create a pause before responding: count to 10; take 3 slow breaths; name the emotion |
| E | Evaluate | Evaluate: "What does this emotion need? What would be a values-consistent response?" |
| → | Act | Take a deliberate, adaptive action (vs. reactive response) |

### Teaching the Chain
1. Introduce and explain each step
2. Practice in session using hypothetical scenarios
3. Role-play with increasing emotional realism
4. Assign real-world practice with self-monitoring
5. Review and reinforce at each session

## Protocol 4: Reinforcement of Adaptive Emotional Responses

Adaptive emotional responses are systematically reinforced to increase their frequency and durability.

| Target Behavior | Reinforcement Type | Schedule | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Using emotion words accurately | Descriptive verbal reinforcement | FR1 initially; fade | "You named that feeling exactly — that's significant" |
| Requesting support instead of withdrawing | Responsive support + verbal reinforcement | FR1 | Natural consequence = connection |
| Using a coping skill before crisis | Acknowledgment + brief discussion of benefit | FR1 | Reinforce prevention, not only crisis response |
| Expressing an emotion to another person | Validation + continuation of conversation | Natural | Natural consequence = felt understanding |
| Staying in session through difficult emotion | Warm verbal acknowledgment | FR1 | "You stayed with that feeling — that took courage" |
| Post-emotional recovery (returning to baseline) | Acknowledgment + discussion | FR1 | Reinforce the recovery process itself |

## Protocol 5: Desensitization and Exposure Framework for Emotional Triggers

### Purpose
To reduce the avoidance of, and distress in response to, specific emotional triggers through systematic exposure paired with new learning.

### Adapted Procedure

**Step 1: Build the emotion trigger hierarchy**
- Identify feared or avoided emotional experiences (not situations, but the emotions themselves)
- Rate each on a subjective distress scale (0–10: SUD)
- Order from least to most distressing

**Step 2: Stabilization and Coping Skill Development**
- Before beginning exposure, ensure the client has at minimum:
  - 3 coping strategies in their repertoire
  - A grounding procedure
  - A self-soothing routine

**Step 3: Graduated Emotional Exposure**
- Begin at the lower end of the hierarchy
- Deliberately evoke or approach the feared emotional experience in controlled conditions
- Maintain exposure until SUD decreases by ≥50% (habituation model) OR until new adaptive behavior is reinforced (inhibitory learning model)
- Process the experience using ACT acceptance work (see act-micropractices.md)

**Step 4: Generalization**
- Practice across different triggering contexts
- Include real-world exposures as the hierarchy progresses

## Protocol 6: Antecedent Modifications for Emotional Regulation

| Antecedent Modification | Target | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Environmental calming | Reduce sensory overwhelm | Dimmed lights; reduced noise; comfortable temperature |
| Emotional check-in at session start | Prime emotional labeling | "On a scale of 0–10, how activated are you right now?" |
| Warning before transitions | Reduce transition-related distress | "In 5 minutes we'll be moving to a harder topic" |
| Psychoeducation about emotion | Reduce fear of emotions | Normalize emotion as information, not threat |
| Establishing emotional safety | Reduce shame inhibition | Explicit relational safety-building before emotional disclosure |
| Regulating therapeutic pacing | Stay within window of tolerance | Slow down when arousal signs appear; speed up when hypoaroused |

## Protocol 7: Crisis Prevention Planning

A behavioral crisis prevention plan identifies early warning signs of emotional crisis, specifies antecedent modifications to prevent escalation, identifies effective interventions at early stages, and establishes a response protocol for full crisis events.

### Crisis Prevention Plan Template

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Early warning signs (Level 1) | [Behavioral indicators of early distress] |
| Level 1 interventions | [Immediate antecedent modifications; coping skill prompting] |
| Escalation signs (Level 2) | [Behavioral indicators of significant distress] |
| Level 2 interventions | [More intensive coping; support activation; session protocol changes] |
| Crisis indicators (Level 3) | [Behavioral indicators of full crisis state] |
| Level 3 response | [Safety protocol; emergency contact; crisis line; hospital protocol] |
| Recovery plan | [What happens after a crisis event; how to return to normal functioning] |

## Trauma-Informed ABA Modifications

- **Replace punishment entirely**: Never use punishment for emotional behaviors — they are communicative, not intentionally oppositional
- **Pacing**: Move more slowly with trauma-exposed clients; use more frequent check-ins; allow more processing time
- **Safety before challenge**: Ensure emotional safety and therapeutic alliance before introducing graduated exposure or FCT
- **Titrated exposure**: Keep exposure well within the window of tolerance; prioritize stabilization when the client is destabilized
- **Positive behavioral support focus**: Frame all intervention in terms of skill-building and functional improvement, never deficit-correction
