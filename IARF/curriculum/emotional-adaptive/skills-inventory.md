# Emotional-Adaptive Dimension: Skills Inventory

> *"The most important thing in communication is hearing what isn't said."*
> — Peter Drucker (adapted for emotional intelligence)

---

## Overview

The Emotional-Adaptive dimension of the IATLAS addresses the capacity to recognize, process, regulate, and express emotions in ways that support resilience, meaningful relationships, and values-aligned action. Emotional intelligence and adaptive emotional functioning are among the most reliably identified factors distinguishing resilient individuals from those who struggle under adversity.

"Emotional" refers to the full range of affective experience — not only positive emotions, but the complete spectrum including grief, fear, anger, shame, and despair. The IATLAS does not privilege positive over negative emotions; all emotions carry information and serve functional purposes. "Adaptive" refers to the capacity to work with emotional experience in ways that support rather than undermine wellbeing and values-aligned living.

This dimension draws from ABA's behavioral approaches to emotional skill-building, ACT's radical acceptance of emotional experience, and the broader emotion regulation literature (Gross, Linehan, Greenberg). Together, these perspectives provide a comprehensive, evidence-based approach to emotional resilience within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

## Core Skill Domains

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **Emotion Recognition** | Accurately identifying and naming emotional states in oneself and others |
| **Emotional Processing** | Engaging with emotional experience in a way that allows it to be metabolized rather than suppressed or amplified |
| **Emotion Regulation** | Modifying the intensity, duration, or behavioral expression of emotional states when appropriate |
| **Adaptive Expression** | Communicating emotional experience in ways that are honest, contextually appropriate, and relationally effective |
| **Emotional Flexibility** | Moving fluidly between emotional states; recovering from emotional intensity; tolerating complex or conflicting emotions |

## Skills Inventory

### Level 1: Foundational Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotion Identification/Labeling** | The ability to accurately identify and name one's own emotional states | Can name at least 8 discrete emotions; distinguishes between primary and secondary emotions; does not rely exclusively on "good/bad/fine" |
| **Body-Emotion Connection** | Recognizing how emotions are experienced in the body; using somatic signals as emotional data | Can describe physical sensations associated with at least 3 emotional states; uses body sensation as an early warning system; does not dissociate emotion from body experience |
| **Basic Coping Strategies** | A repertoire of at least 3–5 strategies for managing emotional distress | Can name and use coping strategies before, during, and after distress; coping strategies are adaptive (not numbing or avoidant) |
| **Emotional Safety** | The sense that one's emotional experience will not destroy or overwhelm — basic psychological containment | Can tolerate moderate levels of emotional distress without crisis; does not exclusively rely on others for emotional regulation; has a sense of internal continuity across emotional states |
| **Distress Tolerance** | The ability to tolerate painful emotional states without acting destructively | Rides out distress for at least 15–20 minutes without impulsive behavior; uses distress tolerance skills (e.g., TIPP, self-soothing) [Research citation needed] |
| **Expressing Feelings Verbally** | The ability to put emotional experience into words in the presence of another person | Uses feeling words accurately; expresses vulnerability without excessive shame; shares emotional experience with at least one trusted person |
| **Recognizing Emotional Triggers** | Identifying the antecedent events, situations, or thoughts that precipitate intense emotional responses | Can identify at least 3 personal emotional triggers; distinguishes triggers from causes; reports recognition of triggers before or as they occur |
| **Self-Soothing** | Behaviors that calm the nervous system and reduce acute emotional distress | Demonstrates at least 3 self-soothing strategies (sensory, physical, cognitive, relational); uses them effectively during distress |

### Level 2: Intermediate Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotion Differentiation** | Distinguishing between similar or co-occurring emotional states with precision | Distinguishes sadness from grief; fear from anxiety; anger from hurt; can hold multiple emotions simultaneously |
| **Processing Difficult Emotions** | Moving through rather than around difficult emotional experiences; grieving, mourning, feeling fear without avoidance | Can sit with difficult emotions for extended periods; completes emotional processing cycles; reports decreased emotional reactivity after processing |
| **Adaptive Coping Selection** | Choosing coping strategies that are appropriate to the situation and state | Matches coping strategy to emotional type and intensity; moves beyond habitual coping toward flexible selection |
| **Emotion Regulation Strategies** | Deliberate use of cognitive reappraisal, behavioral change, attention deployment, or situational modification to regulate emotion | Employs at least 3 distinct regulation strategies; can describe the strategy being used; reports effectiveness |
| **Managing Emotional Intensity** | Modulating the amplitude of emotional experience — neither suppressing nor amplifying beyond functional levels | Maintains functional communication during moderate emotional activation; returns to baseline within a reasonable time frame after intense emotion |
| **Expressing Needs Emotionally** | Communicating emotional needs to others in ways that support connection rather than create distance | States emotional needs directly; avoids blaming or demanding while expressing needs; can tolerate delayed or partial response to stated needs |
| **Supporting Others Emotionally** | Providing emotionally attuned, responsive support to others in distress | Validates others' emotions without minimizing; offers presence before solutions; does not become overwhelmed by others' distress |
| **Post-Emotional Recovery** | Returning to baseline functioning after intense emotional experiences | Recovers to functional baseline within 24 hours of most emotional events; demonstrates shorter recovery time over treatment course |

### Level 3: Advanced Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Complex Emotion Processing** | Working through multi-layered emotional experiences involving grief, moral injury, complicated loss, or prolonged trauma | Engages in sustained emotional processing work without avoidance; integrates complex emotional experience into coherent self-narrative |
| **Emotional Leadership** | Creating emotional safety for others; modeling adaptive emotional expression in group or community contexts | Others seek out this person for emotional support; models non-defensive emotional honesty; contributes to group emotional climate |
| **Facilitating Others' Emotional Work** | Professionally or informally facilitating another person's emotional processing | Uses reflective listening, validation, and appropriate challenge to support another's emotional work; maintains appropriate limits |
| **Emotional Wisdom** | The distilled capacity to draw on one's full emotional history — including suffering — as a source of understanding and insight | Speaks from emotional experience with depth and integration; helps others understand their emotions through well-chosen words; does not avoid or rush past difficult emotional realities |
| **Post-Traumatic Emotional Growth** | Demonstrating emotional capacities that have deepened specifically through processing adversity | Reports expanded emotional range following adversity; demonstrates greater emotional tolerance than pre-trauma baseline; can articulate what adversity taught them emotionally |
| **Transforming Painful Emotions** | Engaging in deliberate work to alchemize difficult emotions (grief, anger, shame) into growth, wisdom, or values-directed energy | Demonstrates specific examples of emotional transformation; can describe the process; does not attempt to skip the painful stage |
| **Emotional Mentorship** | Providing sustained, skilled emotional support and guidance to others navigating emotional difficulty | Provides ongoing emotional mentorship to at least one person; demonstrates patience and skill over time |
| **Teaching Emotional Skills** | Effectively conveying emotional intelligence concepts and practices to others | Explains emotional concepts clearly; adapts to learner's level; creates conditions for emotional learning in others |

## Cross-Dimensional Connections

| Connected Dimension | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Agentic-Generative** | Emotional regulation supports behavioral persistence; emotional processing enables behavioral activation after depression |
| **Relational-Connective** | Emotional expression and empathy are foundational to relational connection; emotional support is a relational skill |
| **Spiritual-Reflective** | Emotion is information about what matters; contemplative practices support emotion regulation; processing suffering is a spiritual task |
| **Somatic-Regulative** | Emotions are embodied; somatic regulation techniques directly support emotional regulation; body-emotion connection is bidirectional |
| **Cognitive-Narrative** | Emotions generate thoughts; thoughts amplify or dampen emotions; narrative reconstruction supports emotional processing |

## Trauma-Informed Considerations

The Emotional-Adaptive dimension requires particular trauma sensitivity:

- **Trauma disrupts emotional regulation**: Many clients have developed emotional dysregulation as an adaptive response to trauma. Approach these presentations without pathologizing.
- **Avoid emotional flooding**: Pacing is critical. Move toward emotional work incrementally; ensure adequate resources before opening emotional material.
- **Window of tolerance**: All emotional work should remain within the client's window of tolerance (Siegel, 1999) [Research citation needed]. Watch for hyper- and hypo-arousal signs.
- **Stabilization first**: For clients with complex trauma, emotional skill-building in the foundation phase is more important than emotional processing.
- **Somatic grounding**: Regularly incorporate somatic-regulative practices to anchor emotional work in the body.

## Assessment Considerations

Baseline emotional assessment should include:

1. **Emotional vocabulary**: Can the client name diverse emotional states?
2. **Somatic awareness**: Does the client connect emotional experience to body sensation?
3. **Regulatory strategies**: What does the client currently do to manage distress?
4. **Trauma history**: What experiences may be relevant to current emotional functioning?
5. **Avoidance patterns**: What emotions is the client most avoiding?
6. **Expression habits**: How does the client typically communicate emotional experience?

**Recommended tools:**
- Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale (DERS; Gratz & Roemer, 2004) [Research citation needed]
- Emotion Regulation Questionnaire (ERQ; Gross & John, 2003) [Research citation needed]
- Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20) for emotion identification difficulties [Research citation needed]
