# Emotional-Adaptive Dimension: Values Exercises

---

## Overview

The relationship between values and emotions is bidirectional and profound. Our emotions signal what we care about — grief tells us we have loved; anger tells us we hold something as important or just; fear tells us something precious is at risk. And our values, in turn, determine how we engage with our emotional lives — whether we approach or avoid, suppress or express, deny or transform our feelings.

The values exercises in the Emotional-Adaptive dimension invite clients to use their emotional experience as a compass to their values, and to use their values as a guide for emotional engagement. These exercises integrate ACT values work with emotion-focused approaches to create a uniquely rich set of tools for emotional resilience within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

## Exercise 1: Emotions as Values Signals

**Purpose**: To help clients understand their difficult emotions as information about what they value, rather than problems to solve or suppress
**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; verbal exploration with optional writing

### Instructions

"I'd like to explore a different way of understanding your difficult emotions — one that starts from the premise that painful emotions are not malfunctions. They are signals.

Let's work through this with your own emotional experience. Think of an emotion you've been experiencing recently that feels difficult or unwanted.

[Client identifies emotion]

Now let's explore it as a signal:

**If this emotion is grief or sadness**: What have you lost, or what do you love that is threatened? What does that tell you about what you value?

**If this emotion is fear or anxiety**: What is at risk? What are you afraid of losing? What does that fear protect?

**If this emotion is anger or frustration**: What value has been violated? What do you consider important or just that is not being honored?

**If this emotion is shame or embarrassment**: What standard or self-image matters to you? What does this say about how you want to be?

**If this emotion is loneliness**: What kind of connection matters to you? What are you longing for?

[Explore the client's specific emotion using the appropriate framing]

So this emotion — as difficult as it is — is actually telling you something about what you value. What is it saying? And what would it mean to honor that value more fully in your life?"

## Exercise 2: What My Emotional History Teaches Me About What Matters

**Purpose**: To mine the client's emotional autobiography for values information; to see one's emotional history as a source of wisdom rather than shame
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written reflection or verbal exploration

### Instructions

"Your emotional history — the full range of what you have felt across your life — is a rich source of information about what matters to you. Even the difficult parts. Especially the difficult parts.

I'd like to invite you to reflect on your emotional history in terms of what it reveals about your values.

Think about the following:
- The losses that hit you hardest — what do they reveal about what you loved?
- The injustices that made you most angry — what do they reveal about what you believe in?
- The moments of greatest joy — what were you doing, with whom, living which values?
- The fears that have most shaped your choices — what were they protecting?
- The shame experiences that left the deepest mark — what standards or identity do they reveal?

Write or speak a paragraph for each: 'My emotional history tells me I value ___________________'"

### Debrief Questions
- "Looking at this, what surprises you?"
- "What values appear most consistently?"
- "How could you honor these values more intentionally?"

## Exercise 3: Emotional Courage Values Work

**Purpose**: To identify the emotional courage required to live one's values; to build willingness to feel difficult emotions in service of what matters
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual

### Instructions

"Living our values takes emotional courage. Acting with honesty requires the courage to tolerate fear of disapproval. Loving deeply requires the courage to tolerate vulnerability. Standing for justice requires the courage to tolerate discomfort and opposition.

Let's explore what emotional courage looks like for you specifically.

For each of your core values, complete this statement:

'To live my value of [value], I need the emotional courage to feel _______________'

For example:
- 'To live my value of authentic connection, I need the courage to feel vulnerable and possibly rejected.'
- 'To live my value of integrity, I need the courage to feel the discomfort of disappointing others.'
- 'To live my value of compassion, I need the courage to feel others' pain without numbing.'

[Work through client's top 3–5 values]

Now: which of these courage requirements feels most pressing right now? And is the courage available — even in small measure?"

## Exercise 4: The Compassionate Witness

**Purpose**: To build self-compassion as a values practice; to reduce the self-critical overlay that amplifies emotional suffering
**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; guided imagery

### Instructions

"This exercise invites you to relate to your own emotional experience the way a truly compassionate witness would — someone who sees your pain fully, does not minimize it, and does not judge you for it.

Close your eyes. Take a few settling breaths.

Bring to mind a recent experience of emotional difficulty — something that hurt, frightened, or overwhelmed you.

[Pause 20 seconds]

Now imagine that you are not alone in this experience. Imagine a compassionate presence beside you — a wise, kind figure who sees everything you're experiencing and holds it with complete acceptance. This figure does not try to fix your pain. They simply bear witness.

What does this compassionate witness see? What do they see in you that you might not let yourself see? What would they say — not to fix, but to recognize?

[Pause 30 seconds]

Now: try to become that compassionate witness toward yourself. See yourself as this figure sees you — fully, without judgment.

What does the compassionate witness want you to know?

[Pause 20 seconds]

Open your eyes."

## Exercise 5: Values in the Storm

**Purpose**: To help clients identify how to maintain values-orientation during periods of intense emotional activation
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual

### Instructions

"In a storm, navigation becomes harder but more important. When we're in emotional turmoil — overwhelmed, flooded, reactive — our values can become difficult to access. This exercise helps you identify your values anchors: the values that can hold steady even in the storm.

Think of a recent or anticipated emotional storm — a period of significant emotional intensity.

Now identify:
1. **The core value that is most at risk during this storm** (the value that the difficult emotion most wants you to abandon — honesty under shame, patience under anger, courage under fear)
2. **The value you most want to stay connected to during the storm** (the value that would guide your behavior if you could access it)
3. **A specific behavior** that would express that value even in the storm — something small, doable, concrete

These three elements are your storm navigation plan: the value at risk, the anchor value, and the anchor behavior.

[Complete the plan in writing or verbally]

What would it mean to hold onto your anchor value in the next difficult emotional moment?"

## Exercise 6: Emotional Legacy — What Emotional Patterns Do I Want to Pass On?

**Purpose**: To connect emotional skills development to legacy values; to provide long-range motivation for emotional growth
**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual

### Instructions

"Our emotional patterns — how we handle pain, express love, manage fear, process anger — are among the most powerful things we transmit to others, particularly the people closest to us.

Reflect on these questions:

**From your family of origin**: What emotional patterns did you inherit? Which do you want to carry forward? Which do you want to transform?

**To future generations or people you influence**: What emotional patterns do you want to model? What do you want children, mentees, or people who look up to you to learn from watching how you handle emotion?

**Your emotional legacy statement**: 'When it comes to emotion, what I want to be known for — and to pass on — is __________________'

And finally: what would you need to change about your current emotional responses to live that legacy more fully?"

## Exercise 7: The Emotional Healing Values Letter

**Purpose**: To connect emotional healing to values; to write a letter of commitment to one's emotional growth
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written exercise

### Instructions

"This final exercise invites you to write a letter — to yourself, or to someone you love — about your commitment to your own emotional healing and growth. Not from obligation, but from values.

Your letter addresses:
1. What you've been through emotionally and what it has cost you
2. What you now understand about yourself that you didn't before
3. The values you want to live from going forward in your emotional life
4. What you commit to — not perfectly, but intentionally
5. What kind of emotional life you are building

This letter is not to be shared unless you choose to. It's a document of your own emotional vision."
