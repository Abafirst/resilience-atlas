# Somatic-Regulative Dimension: ACT Micropractices

---

## Overview

Acceptance & Commitment Therapy has always had a somatic dimension — ACT's mindfulness practices involve the body, and the model of acceptance is fundamentally about learning to tolerate physical discomfort without avoidance. In the Somatic-Regulative dimension, ACT practices take on special importance for two reasons: (1) somatic avoidance (avoiding the body's signals, sensations, and states) is a major maintaining mechanism for both trauma-related and general wellbeing difficulties; and (2) the body is a powerful present-moment anchor for all ACT processes.

The eight practices in this document use the body as a doorway to psychological flexibility — through present-moment awareness, acceptance of physical sensations, defusion from body-critical thoughts, and values clarification in the physical domain.

## Practice 1: The Body Scan Present-Moment Exercise

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: As a session opener; for clients who are dissociated or "in their head"; to establish somatic presence before emotional or cognitive work

### Full Guided Script

"Let's begin by arriving in the room — fully.

Find a comfortable position. Feet flat on the floor, or whatever feels natural. Allow your hands to rest gently. If you're comfortable doing so, let your eyes close; if not, soften your gaze toward the floor.

Begin by taking three full breaths. Not forcing anything — just allowing the breath to deepen a little. With each exhale, you don't have to go anywhere or do anything. You can just be here.

[Pause 15 seconds]

Now let's bring some gentle, curious attention to your body. We're not looking for anything in particular — we're just noticing what's here.

Start at the top of your head. What do you notice? Any sensation, any tightness, any pressure? Just notice — no need to change anything.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Move your attention slowly down to your face. Notice your forehead, your eyes, your jaw. Many of us carry a lot of tension in our jaw and face. Just notice. If there's tension, you don't have to make it go away — just notice that it's there.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Move down to your shoulders and neck. One of the most common places to carry stress. Notice what's here.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Continue down through your arms to your hands. Notice whether your hands are warm or cool, relaxed or tense.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Move your attention to your chest. Notice the movement of your breath here — the rise and fall. Notice whether the chest feels open or constricted. Again — just noticing.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Move to your abdomen. This is often called the second brain — it responds to stress and emotion more directly than any other part of the body. What does your belly feel like right now?

[Pause 10 seconds]

Continue down through your hips and lower back, your thighs, your knees, your calves, and finally to your feet and toes.

[Pause 10 seconds]

Now take a moment to sense your body as a whole — one whole body, here in this room. Breathing. Alive.

What did you notice?

Take one more full breath. Open your eyes when you're ready."

### Clinical Notes
- Modify for trauma history: begin with feet and work up; provide more frequent verbal check-ins; have grounding resource available
- For clients with limited somatic awareness, extended practice across multiple sessions is needed
- Group use: allow 12–15 minutes; invite brief verbal sharing after

## Practice 2: Acceptance of Physical Sensations

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Acceptance
**Best Used When**: Client is fighting, avoiding, or catastrophizing about physical sensations (pain, anxiety physiology, somatic trauma responses)

### Instructions

"Our bodies produce a lot of sensations — many of them pleasant, some of them uncomfortable. One of the ways we create additional suffering is by fighting uncomfortable physical sensations — tensing against pain, dreading anxiety symptoms, trying to force ourselves to relax.

I'd like to try a different approach: bringing an attitude of acceptance to a physical sensation.

[If the client has current physical discomfort]: Let's work with that.
[If not]: Bring to mind a mild, common physical discomfort — tension in the shoulders, tightness in the chest, or the physical feeling of anxiety.

Now, instead of bracing against it or trying to make it go away, try this:

Turn toward the sensation. Approach it with curiosity rather than aversion.

Notice: where is it? What shape does it have? Does it pulse or stay steady? Is it sharp or dull?

Now — and this is the key move — make space for it. Breathe around it, not against it. The sensation can be here. You don't have to like it. You don't have to want it. But you can let it be here.

Notice what happens to the sensation when you stop fighting it."

## Practice 3: Breath as Anchor

**Duration**: 5–10 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Client is dissociated, anxious, or mentally overwhelmed; as a grounding practice at any point in session

### Instructions

"The breath is always happening in the present moment. That makes it one of the most reliable anchors to the now.

We're going to practice using the breath as an anchor — not to control the breath or achieve any particular state, but simply to notice it.

Settle. Take one natural breath.

Now, simply follow your breath with your attention. Notice the sensation of air entering through your nose. Notice the slight pause at the top of the in-breath. Notice the release of the exhale. Notice the brief stillness before the next breath begins.

Your mind will wander. That's not a problem — that's the practice. Every time you notice you've wandered, gently bring your attention back to the breath. No judgment. Just return.

[3–5 minutes of silent practice]

What did you notice? How many times did your mind wander? And what was it like to keep returning?"

## Practice 4: Movement as Values Expression

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes (includes brief movement)
**ACT Processes**: Values, Committed Action, Present-Moment Awareness
**Best Used When**: Connecting physical activity to values; making movement intrinsically meaningful rather than obligatory

### Instructions

"Physical movement is often framed in terms of obligation or outcome: 'I should exercise'; 'I need to lose weight'; 'I have to stay healthy.'

ACT offers a different frame: movement as an expression of values.

I'd like you to think about one kind of movement that has ever felt genuinely meaningful to you — not just healthy, but alive. It could be walking in nature, dancing, swimming, playing with a child, yoga, sport — anything.

What value was that movement expressing? Was it vitality? Presence? Joy? Care for your body? Freedom? Strength?

[Explore]

Now: for the next 5 minutes, let's move in some small way — just here, in this space. It doesn't have to be elaborate. Standing and stretching; walking slowly; swaying slightly. And as you move, bring to mind the value you identified.

Move from that value. Not to exercise. Not to be healthy. But as an expression of something that matters.

[Allow 3–5 minutes of guided movement]

What was that like? Was anything different about movement with a values anchor?"

## Practice 5: Defusion from Body-Critical Thoughts

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Defusion
**Best Used When**: Client has a difficult, critical, or shame-laden relationship with their body

### Common Body-Critical Thoughts and Defusion Responses

| Body-Critical Thought | Defusion Response |
|---|---|
| "My body is broken / wrong" | "My mind is telling me my body is broken. Is that a fact or a story? What does my body actually do, right now?" |
| "I'm disgusting" | "There goes the self-disgust narrative. I've heard this one before. What does my body need from me today — not from that story, but from me?" |
| "I hate my body" | "I notice a thought of hatred. That's a very painful thought. And separately: my body is here, carrying me through this moment." |
| "I'll never be healthy/well" | "My mind is predicting the future. Futures are notoriously hard to predict. What is available to me in this body today?" |
| "My body betrayed me" | "I notice that story. It makes sense given what I've been through. And also: my body kept me alive through that. Both things can be true." |

## Practice 6: The Physical Wellbeing Values Clarification

**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Values, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Beginning somatic dimension work; connecting self-care to values rather than obligation

### Instructions

"Most self-care advice is presented as obligation: you SHOULD sleep 8 hours; you SHOULD exercise; you SHOULD eat well. ACT takes a different approach: what do you CHOOSE, based on what matters to you?

Let me ask you some questions:

What does it mean to you to live in a body that is cared for? What quality of physical life do you value?

If you could describe your relationship with your body in terms of values — not how it looks or performs, but how you engage with it — what would you want that relationship to look like?

What does your physical vitality make possible? What valued activities does your physical health enable?

From these answers: what does caring for your body mean to you at a values level — not an obligation level?"

## Practice 7: Somatic Grounding Meditation

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Present-Moment Awareness, Self-as-Context
**Best Used When**: After a traumatic disclosure or intense emotional processing; when client is flooded or dissociated

### Full Guided Script

"Let's bring you back to the present moment, through your body.

Feel your feet on the floor. Press them down gently. Feel the solidity beneath you. The floor is holding you.

Feel the chair or surface beneath you. Notice its temperature, its texture, its firmness. You are supported.

Take a slow breath in through your nose. Feel the air. On the exhale, let your shoulders drop slightly.

Look around the room. Name five things you can see. [Pause as client names them] Four things you can touch. [Pause] Three things you can hear. [Pause] Two things you can smell. [Pause] One thing you can taste.

You are here. This room is real. This moment is real. Your body is here.

Take one more breath. Notice that even in the middle of difficult experiences, this body — right here, right now — is a ground you can return to.

How are you feeling?"

## Practice 8: Willingness and Physical Discomfort

**Duration**: 10–15 minutes
**ACT Processes**: Acceptance, Committed Action
**Best Used When**: Client is avoiding physical activity, somatic practices, or healthcare due to anticipated or actual physical discomfort

### Instructions

"We often avoid not just emotional discomfort, but physical discomfort. We don't exercise because it's tiring. We don't attend to our body because it hurts or scares us. We don't do the body scan because we might feel something we'd rather not.

ACT willingness applies to physical discomfort as much as emotional discomfort.

I'd like to ask you: what physical experience are you most avoiding right now? Is it physical activity? A medical appointment? A somatic practice?

[Identify avoidance]

What would be required — physically — to engage with this? What discomfort might show up?

And: are you willing to experience that discomfort while acting in service of your physical wellbeing values?

Willingness with physical discomfort isn't masochism — it's choosing to prioritize what matters over short-term avoidance of discomfort. The body can tolerate more than the mind usually predicts."

## Trauma-Sensitive Somatic ACT Adaptations

| Modification | Application |
|---|---|
| Always offer choice | "You can have eyes open or closed" — never direct |
| Provide external orientation option | "Feel the floor; look around the room" before internal attention |
| Slower pacing | More pauses; more verbal check-ins; explicitly slower transitions |
| Avoid anatomy-specific language for trauma-relevant body parts | Use client's language; skip body parts if client requests |
| Always close with external grounding | End every somatic practice with a return to external orientation |
| Gradual introduction | Build somatic tolerance across multiple sessions before extended practices |
