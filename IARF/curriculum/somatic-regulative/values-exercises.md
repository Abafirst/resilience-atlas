# Somatic-Regulative Dimension: Values Exercises

---

## Overview

The values exercises in the Somatic-Regulative dimension invite clients to explore what physical vitality, bodily care, and somatic wellbeing mean to them — not as obligations, but as expressions of what they most deeply value. For many clients, particularly those who have experienced illness, trauma, disability, or chronic pain, developing a values-based relationship with the body is transformative.

These exercises draw on the ACT tradition of values clarification, somatic psychology, and narrative approaches to body experience. They are designed to be accessible across diverse body histories, physical capacities, and cultural backgrounds.

---

## Exercise 1: What Does Physical Vitality Mean to Me?

**Purpose**: To clarify the client's own definition of physical vitality and connect it to their values
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written reflection or verbal exploration

### Instructions

"Physical vitality looks different for everyone. For one person, it means running marathons. For another, it means having enough energy to play with their children. For someone living with chronic illness, it might mean moving through the day with dignity and minimal pain.

There's no universal standard here — only what's true for you.

I'd like to ask you: what does physical vitality mean to you, in your life, in your body?

Consider:
- When do you feel most physically alive or well?
- What does your body feel like at its best? (Describe in sensory, specific terms)
- What physical state enables you to live most fully from your values?
- What physical capacities matter most to you? Why?

Write or speak your own definition of physical vitality: 'For me, physical vitality means ___________________'"

### Debrief Questions
- "How does your definition compare to cultural messages about health and fitness?"
- "How well does your current physical life match your definition?"
- "What one change would bring you closer to your version of vitality?"

## Exercise 2: My Body's Story

**Purpose**: To construct a narrative account of the client's relationship with their body; to honor the body's history and resilience
**Duration**: 30–40 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written or verbal

### Instructions

"Your body has a story. It has carried you through everything you've experienced — the joys, the challenges, the traumas, the ordinary days. It has adapted, healed, changed, and endured.

I'd like to invite you to tell me your body's story — in three parts:

**Part 1: My earliest body memories**
What is your earliest memory of being in your body? What was it like to inhabit your body as a child?

**Part 2: The body through difficulty**
What has your body been through? What challenges, illnesses, injuries, or threats has it faced? What did it do? How did it survive?

**Part 3: What my body has given me**
What has your body made possible? What experiences, relationships, achievements, or moments of beauty have happened because you have a body?

Finally: what do you want to say to your body — honestly, perhaps for the first time?"

### Facilitator Notes
- This exercise can be deeply emotional, particularly for clients with chronic illness, disability, or somatic trauma
- Honor grief, anger, and complicated feelings about the body — do not rush to gratitude
- The goal is relationship with the body, not body positivity (which can feel coercive)

## Exercise 4: Movement as Metaphor

**Purpose**: To explore what movement means within the client's broader values context; to connect physical movement to life meaning
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; can include brief guided movement

### Instructions

"Movement is not only physical — it carries meaning. When we walk toward something, we express direction. When we stretch, we express expansion. When we rest, we express trust.

I'd like to explore what movement means within your life.

Think of a type of movement that feels meaningful to you — walking, swimming, dancing, playing, stretching. It doesn't have to be elaborate.

What is this movement an expression of in your life? What metaphor does it carry?

For example:
- Walking might express direction, exploration, or presence
- Dancing might express joy, freedom, or connection
- Stretching might express opening up, becoming more
- Resting might express trust, acceptance, or completion

What does your chosen movement say about how you want to move through your life?

And conversely: are there ways your physical movement patterns reflect patterns in your psychological life? Do you tend to be still when you should be moving? To move when you should rest?"

## Exercise 5: The Rest and Recovery Values Letter

**Purpose**: To connect rest and recovery to values; to address the common cultural devaluation of rest
**Duration**: 20–30 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; written exercise

### Instructions

"In our culture, rest is often treated as laziness, and recovery is seen as a concession to weakness. But from a values perspective, rest and recovery are acts of wisdom and care — they are what make sustained, meaningful action possible.

I'd like you to write a brief letter to yourself about rest. Address:

1. What you've been taught about rest (messages from family, culture, religion about whether it's okay to rest)
2. What rest actually is for you — what it feels like when you truly allow yourself to rest
3. What value is expressed by allowing yourself genuine recovery — is it self-compassion? Sustainable living? Presence?
4. A commitment: how you want to honor rest in your life going forward"

## Exercise 6: Body Gratitude Practice

**Purpose**: To cultivate appreciation for the body's capacities and resilience; to shift the relationship from critical to grateful
**Duration**: 15–20 minutes
**Modality**: Individual; can be adapted as an ongoing daily practice

### Instructions

"Gratitude practices are evidence-based wellbeing interventions. Body gratitude is a specific form: deliberately noticing and acknowledging what your body does for you.

This is not about denying pain or difficulty — it can hold both.

For each area below, identify one specific thing your body does that you are genuinely grateful for:

| Body System | What I'm Grateful For |
|---|---|
| My hands | |
| My legs and feet | |
| My senses (sight, hearing, touch, smell, taste) | |
| My immune system | |
| My heart and lungs | |
| My nervous system | |
| My capacity for pleasure | |
| My capacity for healing | |

Now: is there one thing your body does — one capacity, one survival, one small ordinary miracle — that you have never stopped to appreciate?

Tell me about it."

## Exercise 7: Physical Legacy — What Do I Want My Physical Life to Demonstrate?

**Purpose**: To connect physical self-care to legacy values; to find long-range motivation for somatic wellbeing
**Duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Modality**: Individual

### Instructions

"Physical legacy is about more than longevity — it's about what your embodied life demonstrates to others and what it makes possible.

Reflect on:
- What do you want your physical choices to model for the people who watch you — children, younger generations, peers?
- What physical capacity do you most want to maintain so you can continue doing what matters to you?
- When people remember your physical presence — your energy, your movement, your aliveness — what do you want them to remember?
- What physical practices or disciplines do you want to pass on?

Complete this statement: 'Through how I inhabit and care for my body, I want to demonstrate ___________________'"
