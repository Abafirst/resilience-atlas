# Somatic-Regulative Dimension: Skills Inventory

> *"The body keeps the score."*
> — Bessel van der Kolk, *The Body Keeps the Score*

---

## Overview

The Somatic-Regulative dimension of the IARF addresses the body as a fundamental resilience resource. Decades of research in trauma, neuroscience, and behavioral medicine have converged on a simple truth: physical health, bodily awareness, and somatic regulation are not peripheral to psychological resilience — they are foundational to it.

"Somatic" refers to the body as a living, sensing, responding system — not merely a vessel for the mind, but an integral part of how we experience, process, and respond to life. "Regulative" refers to the capacity to maintain and restore physiological equilibrium — to manage arousal, recover from stress, and sustain physical wellbeing under conditions of challenge.

The IARF's Somatic-Regulative dimension draws from behavioral approaches to physical health, somatic psychology, trauma-informed body work, and mindfulness-based stress reduction. Together, these traditions offer a rich, evidence-based curriculum for building bodily resilience within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

This dimension is critically important for trauma-exposed populations, for whom the body is often the primary site of suffering and, ultimately, of healing.

## Core Skill Domains

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **Body Awareness** | The capacity to accurately perceive and interpret physical sensations, arousal states, and somatic signals |
| **Breath Regulation** | Deliberate use of breathing to regulate physiological arousal and support emotional equilibrium |
| **Movement & Mobility** | Engagement with physical movement as a regulation, wellbeing, and resilience practice |
| **Physical Self-Care** | Behavioral repertoire for maintaining physical health through nutrition, sleep, hydration, and preventive care |
| **Rest & Recovery** | The capacity to engage in adequate physical rest and recovery from stress and exertion |

## Skills Inventory

### Level 1: Foundational Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic Body Scanning** | The ability to systematically attend to physical sensations throughout the body | Can complete a brief body scan (5–10 minutes) without excessive distress; reports at least 3 distinct physical sensations; does not skip or rush the scan |
| **Diaphragmatic Breathing** | The ability to breathe using the diaphragm rather than the chest; slower, deeper respiration | Demonstrates observable diaphragmatic breathing on instruction; can practice for at least 5 continuous minutes; reports calming effect |
| **Identifying Physical Stress Signals** | Recognizing the physical signs that indicate physiological stress or arousal | Can name at least 3 personal physical stress signals (e.g., tension headache, tight shoulders, racing heart); identifies these in real-time |
| **Sleep Hygiene Basics** | Understanding and practicing fundamental sleep hygiene behaviors | Can name at least 5 evidence-based sleep hygiene practices; reports consistent sleep schedule; has reduced phone/screen use before bed |
| **Hydration and Nutrition Awareness** | Attention to the relationship between physical inputs (water, food) and energy, mood, and functioning | Reports regular hydration; recognizes hunger and fullness cues; can describe basic nutritional awareness without being prescriptive |
| **Physical Rest Recognition** | Accurately identifying when the body needs rest and responding adaptively | Can distinguish between physical fatigue and emotional tiredness; responds to physical fatigue by resting rather than pushing through |
| **Simple Stretching/Movement** | Engagement in basic stretching and gentle movement as a daily regulation practice | Completes at least 5 minutes of stretching or gentle movement per day; reports body awareness during movement |
| **Grounding Techniques** | Use of sensory or physical techniques to return attention to the present moment via the body | Can demonstrate at least 2 somatic grounding techniques (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1 sensory, foot-on-floor); uses them during periods of dissociation or high arousal |

### Level 2: Intermediate Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Progressive Muscle Relaxation** | Systematic tensing and releasing of muscle groups to produce a deep relaxation response | Can complete a full 20-minute PMR protocol independently; reports significant reduction in somatic tension; practices ≥3 times/week |
| **Paced Breathing** | Use of timed breathing patterns (e.g., 4-7-8, box breathing, 5-5 pace) to shift physiological state | Can demonstrate at least 2 paced breathing techniques; uses them adaptively during arousal; reports measurable calming effect |
| **Movement as Regulation** | Using physical activity — walking, dancing, yoga, sport — as a deliberate emotion and arousal regulation strategy | Reports using movement as a response to stress or emotional difficulty; has a regular movement practice; can describe the regulation effect |
| **Physical Activity Scheduling** | Building regular physical activity into daily or weekly routine using behavioral scheduling techniques | Has a consistent physical activity schedule (≥3 times/week); maintains activity schedule despite mood fluctuation |
| **Recovery Planning** | Deliberately planning and protecting physical recovery time after demanding periods | Builds recovery time into schedule after intense periods; does not consistently sacrifice recovery for productivity; sleeps ≥7 hours consistently |
| **Pain Management Basics** | Basic non-pharmacological pain management skills (heat/cold, movement, breathing, pacing) | Can describe and use at least 3 non-pharmacological pain management strategies; uses pacing to manage chronic pain or physical limitation |
| **Somatic Awareness in Stress** | Using somatic signals as real-time data about stress levels during challenging situations | Reports noticing somatic stress signals before becoming overwhelmed; uses somatic awareness to regulate behavior in challenging contexts |
| **Body-Mind Connection** | Understanding and leveraging the bidirectional relationship between physical state and psychological state | Can describe at least 2 examples of body affecting mind and 2 of mind affecting body; uses this understanding to inform self-care |

### Level 3: Advanced Skills

| Skill | Definition | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Advanced Somatic Practices** | Regular engagement with sophisticated somatic practices (yoga, qigong, somatics, MBSR, etc.) | Maintains a regular advanced somatic practice; demonstrates depth of engagement; can describe the specific benefits |
| **Athletic/Physical Resilience** | The capacity to push physical limits purposefully and recover effectively; physical competence as a resilience resource | Engages in physically demanding activity and recovers well; uses physical challenge as a values expression and confidence builder |
| **Teaching Body Awareness** | Effectively guiding others through basic body awareness practices | Can lead a body scan or breathing practice for others; adapts guidance to others' capacity; demonstrates patience with somatic discomfort in others |
| **Trauma-Sensitive Movement** | Engaging in and facilitating movement practices that are specifically adapted for trauma-exposed individuals | Demonstrates awareness of trauma responses in movement contexts; practices or facilitates in trauma-sensitive manner; can identify contraindications |
| **Physical Health Advocacy** | Actively seeking and maintaining appropriate medical care; navigating healthcare systems effectively | Maintains regular medical appointments; communicates health needs effectively with providers; advocates for appropriate care |
| **Somatic Integration** | The capacity to integrate somatic experience into a coherent, embodied sense of self | Reports feeling "at home" in their body; can describe their physical life with depth and connection; body is experienced as a resource, not only a site of pain |
| **Mind-Body Practices** | Regular engagement with integrative practices that explicitly cultivate mind-body connection (MBSR, somatic experiencing, biofeedback) | Maintains a mind-body practice; demonstrates understanding of the practice's mechanism; reports specific, ongoing benefits |
| **Physical Mentorship** | Supporting others in developing their physical self-care and somatic regulation skills | Models physical self-care; shares knowledge and practices with others; demonstrates patience and non-coerciveness |

## Special Populations Considerations

### Chronic Illness and Physical Disability
- Adapt all skills to the individual's physical capacity; never assume universal movement ability
- Somatic awareness may need to explicitly include learning to distinguish pain signals from stress signals
- Rest and recovery may take on different meanings; work collaboratively with medical providers
- Honor the body as worthy of care even when it is painful, limited, or changed by illness

### Trauma and the Body
- Many trauma survivors experience their body as dangerous or alien; begin with basic safety and containment
- Avoid touch-based practices without explicit consent; honor somatic boundaries
- Body scanning may activate trauma memories; proceed slowly and with grounding resources available
- Somatic regulation is often the essential foundation for any higher-level trauma work

### Cultural Considerations
- Cultural norms around the body, physical activity, and self-care vary significantly
- Avoid prescribing specific physical activities that are culturally incongruent
- In some cultures, body-based practices carry spiritual or sacred significance; approach with respect
- Access to physical activity facilities, healthy food, and healthcare is shaped by socioeconomic context

## Cross-Dimensional Connections

| Connected Dimension | Nature of Connection |
|---|---|
| **Emotional-Adaptive** | Somatic regulation is the physiological foundation of emotion regulation; body awareness underlies emotional awareness |
| **Agentic-Generative** | Physical energy and vitality support behavioral activation and initiative; fatigue is a barrier to purposeful action |
| **Relational-Connective** | Co-regulation — two nervous systems settling together — is the foundation of secure attachment; physical presence supports connection |
| **Spiritual-Reflective** | Many contemplative practices are embodied (walking meditation, yoga, breath prayer); the body is often the site of spiritual experience |
| **Cognitive-Narrative** | Somatic states influence cognitive processing; body-based regulation can interrupt maladaptive cognitive patterns |

## Assessment Considerations

Baseline somatic assessment should include:

1. **Current physical activity level**: Frequency, duration, and type
2. **Sleep patterns**: Duration, quality, regularity
3. **Somatic awareness**: Can the client attend to physical sensations without distress?
4. **Physical stress responses**: What does the client's body do under stress?
5. **Medical history**: Relevant physical health conditions that may affect somatic work
6. **Body history and relationship**: How does the client relate to their body? Is it experienced as ally, enemy, or object?
7. **Trauma in the body**: Has the body been the site of trauma? Is there chronic pain, dissociation, or somatic symptoms?
