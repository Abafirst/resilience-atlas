# Somatic-Regulative Dimension: ABA Protocols

---

## Overview

Applied Behavior Analysis offers highly effective tools for establishing and maintaining the physical self-care behaviors, somatic regulation practices, and health behaviors that constitute the Somatic-Regulative dimension of the IARF. The challenge in somatic work is not a lack of knowledge — most clients know that sleep, movement, and breath regulation are beneficial. The challenge is behavioral: establishing these practices as consistent, self-sustaining habits that persist through periods of low motivation, stress, and life disruption.

ABA's strengths — habit formation, behavioral chaining, reinforcement, antecedent modification, and data-driven adjustment — are precisely what is needed to bridge the gap between knowing and doing in the somatic domain. The protocols in this document apply these tools systematically within The Resilience Atlas™ framework.

## Protocol 1: Relaxation Training Protocol (Jacobson Progressive Muscle Relaxation, Adapted)

### Purpose
To teach systematic deep muscle relaxation as a somatic regulation skill; to establish PMR as a regular, self-directed practice.

### Theoretical Basis
Jacobson (1938) developed PMR based on the principle that muscle tension is the physiological substrate of psychological stress, and that deliberately inducing relaxation produces a parasympathetic nervous system response that counteracts the stress response. Decades of research have confirmed PMR's efficacy across anxiety, PTSD, pain, insomnia, and general stress [Research citation needed].

### Full PMR Protocol (Adapted for Resilience Context)

**Total duration**: 20–25 minutes
**Position**: Seated or lying down; comfortable, quiet environment
**Contraindications**: Significant physical injury in any muscle group (skip that group); severe trauma activation (use shorter version; maintain grounding)

**Preparation**:
"Find a comfortable position. Loosen any tight clothing. Allow your eyes to close or soften your gaze. Take three slow, deep breaths, and with each exhale, allow your body to begin settling."

**Sequence** (tense for 5–7 seconds; release and rest 30–40 seconds; notice the contrast):
1. Hands and forearms — make a fist; release
2. Upper arms — flex biceps; release
3. Forehead — raise eyebrows; release
4. Eyes and nose — scrunch tightly; release
5. Cheeks and jaw — bite down gently; release
6. Neck and throat — gently press head back; release
7. Shoulders — raise to ears; release
8. Upper back — pull shoulders back; release
9. Chest — deep breath in and hold; release with exhale
10. Abdomen — tighten stomach muscles; release
11. Lower back — gently arch; release
12. Thighs — press knees together; release
13. Calves — flex feet back toward shins; release
14. Feet and toes — curl toes; release

**Closing**:
"Take a moment to scan your entire body, noticing the difference between tension and release. Your body knows how to relax. This skill belongs to you."

### ABA Teaching Protocol

**Phase 1** (Sessions 1–2): Clinician-guided full PMR; client practices with complete verbal instruction
**Phase 2** (Sessions 3–4): Clinician provides abbreviated prompts; client fills in independently
**Phase 3** (Sessions 5–6): Client practices with minimal verbal support; clinician observes
**Phase 4** (Home practice): Client practices independently 5x/week; tracks using habit log
**Mastery criterion**: Independent completion of abbreviated PMR (10 minutes) ≥4x/week for 4 consecutive weeks

## Protocol 2: Behavioral Chain for Physical Self-Care Routine

### Purpose
To establish a daily physical self-care routine as a behavioral chain — a connected sequence of behaviors that flows from a single antecedent event.

### Sample Morning Self-Care Behavioral Chain

| Step | Behavior | Estimated Duration |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Wake at consistent time (SD: alarm) | 0 min |
| 2 | Drink a full glass of water | 1 min |
| 3 | Brief body scan (lying in bed) | 3 min |
| 4 | 5 minutes of stretching | 5 min |
| 5 | Hygiene routine | 10 min |
| 6 | Nutritious breakfast | 15 min |
| 7 | 5 minutes of diaphragmatic breathing | 5 min |
| 8 | Physical activity (walk, exercise, movement) | 20–30 min |

**Total**: approximately 60 minutes

### Teaching the Chain
1. Introduce the chain as a sequence, not a collection of separate tasks
2. Begin with the behaviors already in the client's repertoire; add new behaviors one at a time
3. Use forward chaining (teach from step 1) or backward chaining (teach final step first, gradually add preceding steps) based on client's profile
4. Track chain completion using a daily checklist
5. Reinforce each completed step; reinforce chain completion most richly

## Protocol 3: Habit Formation for Somatic Practices

### Purpose
To establish specific somatic practices as self-sustaining habits through systematic behavior change principles.

### The Habit Loop Applied to Somatic Practices

| Element | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Cue (SD) | Environmental signal that triggers the behavior | Finishing morning coffee |
| Routine | The target somatic behavior | 10-minute body scan meditation |
| Reward | Reinforcer that consolidates the habit | Note in habit journal; sense of grounded calm |

### Habit Formation Steps
1. Select one somatic practice to establish
2. Start extremely small (2–3 minutes; once per day)
3. Anchor to an existing routine (habit stacking)
4. Create or identify a clear environmental cue
5. Remove friction (have mat rolled out; journal on nightstand)
6. Track on a simple habit log
7. Increase duration gradually (2 min → 5 min → 10 min over 4–6 weeks)
8. Allow natural reinforcers to take over from external tracking

## Protocol 4: Activity Scheduling and Behavioral Activation (Physical Domain)

### Purpose
To increase physical activity frequency and regularity through systematic scheduling, reinforcement, and barrier removal.

### Procedure

**Step 1: Activity Assessment**
- Baseline physical activity monitoring (IPAQ or movement tracker, 7 days)
- Identify current activity type, frequency, duration, enjoyment
- Identify barriers and facilitators

**Step 2: Values Alignment**
- What does physical activity mean to the client? What values does it express?
- Connect activity scheduling to values (not only "because it's good for me")

**Step 3: Activity Hierarchy**
- Create graded activity list from easy/low effort to challenging
- Include activities that are intrinsically enjoyable, not only health-oriented

**Step 4: Activity Prescription**
- Schedule specific physical activities: day, time, duration, location
- Use implementation intentions for each activity
- Begin with activities from the bottom of the hierarchy

**Step 5: Monitoring and Reinforcement**
- Review activity log weekly
- Reinforce completion; problem-solve non-completion without shame
- Gradually increase challenge over 4–8 weeks

## Protocol 5: Stimulus Control for Rest and Sleep

### Purpose
To improve sleep quality and physical rest through systematic antecedent modification.

### Sleep Stimulus Control Protocol

Based on evidence-based Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) principles [Research citation needed]:

| Behavior | Instruction |
|---|---|
| Bed use | Use the bed only for sleep (and sex); no phones, screens, work, or worrying in bed |
| Consistent sleep time | Rise at the same time every day, regardless of sleep quality the night before |
| Stimulus narrowing | Establish pre-sleep routine (same sequence each night) as an SD for sleep |
| Light management | Bright light upon waking; dim light in the 2 hours before sleep |
| Temperature | Cool sleep environment (65–68°F / 18–20°C) |
| Stimulant management | No caffeine after noon; no alcohol within 3 hours of sleep |
| Pre-sleep wind-down | 20–30 minute low-arousal activity before bed (reading, gentle stretching, PMR) |

|---|---|---|

## Protocol 7: Shaping Physical Activity Levels

For sedentary or deconditioned clients, shaping is essential — reinforcing successive approximations toward a meaningful physical activity level.

| Shaping Step | Behavior | Criterion Before Advancing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 5-minute daily walk | 7 consecutive days |
| 2 | 10-minute daily walk | 7 consecutive days |
| 3 | 20-minute daily walk | 7 consecutive days |
| 4 | 30-minute daily walk or equivalent | 2 weeks |
| 5 | Add second activity type (stretching, swimming, cycling) | 2 weeks |
| 6 | Structured exercise 3–4x/week | 4 weeks |
| 7 | Varied activity schedule with rest days | Ongoing maintenance |

## Trauma-Informed Somatic ABA

- **Explicit consent for any body-focused practice**: Obtain ongoing, specific consent
- **Client controls the pace**: Never push somatic work faster than the client is ready
- **Build a foundation of safety**: Somatic regulation work requires felt safety in the relationship first
- **Trauma-sensitive movement**: In group settings, always provide options; never require specific poses or positions
- **Watch for dissociation**: Have grounding procedures immediately available during all somatic practices
- **Honor the body's history**: Some bodies carry significant physical histories (illness, injury, abuse); approach with deep respect
