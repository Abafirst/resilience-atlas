# Cultural Adaptations for IATLAS Activities

## Core Principles

### 1. Cultural Humility
Approach every family as the expert on their own culture. Practitioners bring clinical expertise; families bring cultural expertise. Both are essential.

### 2. Asset-Based Lens
Identify and build on cultural strengths. Every cultural background contains unique resilience resources — stories, practices, relationships, values — that can be amplified.

### 3. Collaborative Adaptation
Co-create adaptations *with* families, not *for* them. Ask: "How would this activity look in your family?" rather than prescribing modifications.

### 4. Flexibility First
Every IATLAS activity can be meaningfully adapted. When in doubt, ask the family. Their cultural wisdom will always improve the activity.

## Language and Communication

### Working with Interpreters
- Brief interpreter before session on clinical vocabulary
- Allow extra time — sessions with interpretation typically take 50% longer
- Direct questions to the client, not the interpreter
- Check understanding frequently: "Can you tell me in your own words what we're doing?"

### Emotion Vocabulary
Many languages do not have direct equivalents for English emotion words. Work collaboratively to find culturally resonant terms.

| English Term | Approach |
|-------------|----------|
| "Anxiety"   | Ask: "What word does your family use when something worries you?" |
| "Mindfulness" | Frame as "paying attention" or connect to existing meditation/prayer practices |
| "Boundaries" | In collectivist cultures, frame as "how we show respect and care" |
| "Self-care"  | In many cultures, reframe as "caring well for your family by caring for yourself" |

### Written Materials
- Simplify to Grade 5–6 reading level for all parent-facing materials
- Provide audio versions for low-literacy families
- Use images alongside text wherever possible

## Material Substitutions

| Original Material | Cultural Alternatives |
|------------------|----------------------|
| Crayons | Coloured pencils, chalk, natural dyes, charcoal, fabric markers |
| Western paper | Sand tray, digital tablet, chalkboard, fabric, leaves |
| Board games | Traditional games from client's culture |
| Journals | Voice recording, video diary, art portfolio |
| "Therapy office" props | Familiar home objects |

## Activity Adaptation Examples

### "Gratitude Journal" → "Gratitude Practice"

**Western default**: Written journal with daily entries

**Oral tradition cultures**: Audio recording, storytelling circle, spoken gratitude at meals

**Collectivist cultures**: Family gratitude sharing where each member names something they appreciate about the family

**Indigenous cultures**: Gratitude to the land, ancestors, and community; connection to place and seasonal cycles

**Religious contexts**: Gratitude prayer, acknowledgement of blessings within faith tradition

---

### "My Goals Treasure Map" → "My Path Forward"

**Western default**: Visual map with individual goals

**Confucian cultures**: Frame goals in terms of contribution to family/community honour

**Collectivist cultures**: Include family and community goals alongside personal ones; seek elder guidance

**Indigenous cultures**: Connect goals to cultural identity, land, and responsibility to future generations

**Oral cultures**: Create a spoken "story of where I am going" rather than a written/drawn map

---

### "Body Scan Meditation" → "Body Awareness Practice"

**Religious contexts**: Many traditions have existing body-awareness practices (e.g., Islamic prayer positions, yoga, Christian laying on of hands). Connect to these.

**Trauma contexts**: Introduce slowly. Some clients experience body awareness as threatening. Start with external, then progress to internal sensation.

**Cultural modesty**: Respect cultural norms around touching one's own body, especially for adolescents. Offer fully clothed, movement-based alternatives.

**Sensory differences**: Provide choice between different body-awareness approaches.

## Family Structure Considerations

| Family Structure | Adaptation |
|-----------------|------------|
| Single-parent | "Safe person" activities should include extended family, community members, teachers |
| Multi-generational | Include grandparents as wisdom-keepers in activities; acknowledge their role |
| Foster/adoptive | Adjust "family history" activities; focus on current connections and chosen family |
| LGBTQ+ families | Use inclusive language; ensure all family configurations are normalised |
| Blended families | Include all parental figures; be sensitive to loyalty conflicts |

## Religious and Spiritual Adaptations

### General Approach
- Never assume or impose religious framing
- Ask: "Does your family have any spiritual or religious practices that are important to you?"
- Connect resilience concepts to existing faith practices where invited

### Examples

**"Gratitude" in religious contexts**:
- Islamic: Connect to *shukr* (gratitude to Allah)
- Jewish: Connect to *hakarat hatov* (recognising the good)
- Christian: Connect to thankfulness and prayer
- Buddhist: Connect to appreciation and non-attachment
- Hindu: Connect to *seva* (service) and *satsang* (community)

**"Mindfulness" in religious contexts**:
- Connect to existing contemplative practices (prayer, meditation, chanting)
- Use the family's existing language: "quiet time," "being present with God," "centering"
- Avoid language that conflicts with beliefs about consciousness or the self

## Self-Assessment for Practitioners

Before each session with a family from a different cultural background, consider:

1. What do I know about this family's cultural background?
2. What assumptions might I be bringing?
3. What do I need to ask to understand their cultural context?
4. How might I need to adapt this activity?
5. Who can I consult if I'm unsure? (Supervisor, cultural consultant, community liaison)

---

## Asking Good Questions

Use these questions to guide cultural adaptation:

- "In your family/community, how do people usually handle [situation]?"
- "Is there a word in your language that captures what we're trying to do here?"
- "How does this activity feel to you? Is there anything that doesn't fit for your family?"
- "What would make this activity feel more like something your family would do at home?"
- "Are there any parts of this that feel uncomfortable or inappropriate given your background?"
