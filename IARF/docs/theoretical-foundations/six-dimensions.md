# The Six Dimensions of Resilience

> *Resilience is not a single trait or a fixed quantity. It is a multi-dimensional capacity — expressed differently across different domains of a person's life.*

---

## Overview

The six-dimension model of resilience was established through original doctoral research (Molchany, 2013) using qualitative analysis of 18 resilience exemplars. Each dimension represents a distinct, validated domain in which resilience is expressed, built, and measured.

The six dimensions are:
1. **Agentic-Generative**
2. **Relational-Connective**
3. **Spiritual-Reflective**
4. **Emotional-Adaptive**
5. **Somatic-Regulative**
6. **Cognitive-Narrative**

These dimensions are **interdependent** — growth in one dimension often catalyzes growth in others — but they are **distinct** enough to be assessed, targeted, and measured separately.

---

## Dimension 1: Agentic-Generative

### Definition
The capacity to take purposeful action, exercise self-efficacy, and generate forward momentum in the face of adversity.

### Core Constructs
- **Self-efficacy**: Belief in one's capacity to act and influence outcomes
- **Agency**: Sense of being the author of one's own life
- **Goal-directed behavior**: Purposeful action oriented toward meaningful outcomes
- **Generativity**: Creating value, contributing to others, leaving a legacy

### Behavioral Indicators
- Sets and pursues goals even under stress
- Takes initiative rather than waiting for circumstances to change
- Maintains a sense of purpose and direction during adversity
- Finds ways to contribute and create, even in limited circumstances

### Barriers to Agentic Resilience
- Learned helplessness (history of uncontrollable aversive events)
- Cognitive fusion with narratives of powerlessness
- Lack of reinforcement for self-directed action
- Environmental constraints that have historically blocked agency

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Behavioral activation, goal-setting with successive approximation
- Values clarification and committed action (ACT)
- Building mastery experiences that strengthen self-efficacy
- Shaping forward-directed behavior

---

## Dimension 2: Relational-Connective

### Definition
The capacity to access, cultivate, and maintain meaningful social connection as a source of resilience during adversity.

### Core Constructs
- **Social support**: Tangible, emotional, and informational support from relationships
- **Belonging**: A felt sense of connection and acceptance
- **Reciprocity**: Mutual support and contribution in relationships
- **Help-seeking**: The behavioral capacity to ask for and accept support

### Behavioral Indicators
- Reaches out to others during times of stress
- Maintains relationships under pressure
- Accepts support without shame or avoidance
- Offers support to others (reciprocity in relationships)

### Barriers to Relational Resilience
- Avoidance of vulnerability (especially in those with attachment trauma)
- Social skills deficits that make connection difficult
- Beliefs that "burdening" others is wrong or shameful
- Lack of available social supports in the environment

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Social skills training (modeling, rehearsal, feedback)
- Acceptance of vulnerability and connection (ACT)
- Relationship mapping and network development
- Communication and interpersonal effectiveness skills

---

## Dimension 3: Spiritual-Reflective

### Definition
The capacity to find meaning, purpose, and existential grounding — through values, beliefs, practices, or connection to something larger than oneself.

### Core Constructs
- **Meaning-making**: Constructing meaning from experience, including adversity
- **Values**: Clear, lived commitments that guide action
- **Transcendence**: Connection to something beyond the immediate self
- **Reflective practice**: Regular engagement with inner life and deeper questions

### Behavioral Indicators
- Articulates personal values and acts in alignment with them
- Finds or constructs meaning even in painful experiences
- Engages in reflective practices (journaling, prayer, meditation, community)
- Maintains a sense of purpose even when circumstances are difficult

### Barriers to Spiritual Resilience
- Moral injury or faith crisis following trauma
- Absence of practiced reflective habits
- Values that have never been explicitly clarified
- Existential avoidance (refusing to engage with deeper questions)

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Values clarification (ACT) as the primary intervention
- Reflective journaling and practice-building
- Meaning-making exercises (narrative work, benefit-finding)
- Building behavioral habits of reflection and spiritual practice

---

## Dimension 4: Emotional-Adaptive

### Definition
The capacity to experience, process, and adaptively respond to the full range of emotional experience — especially during adversity.

### Core Constructs
- **Emotional awareness**: Identifying and labeling one's emotional states
- **Emotional acceptance**: Willingness to feel difficult emotions without avoidance
- **Emotional flexibility**: Shifting emotional responses as contexts change
- **Adaptive expression**: Expressing emotions in ways that serve relationships and goals

### Behavioral Indicators
- Can name what they are feeling, even under stress
- Does not avoid or suppress difficult emotions
- Can shift emotional state when the situation calls for it
- Expresses emotions in ways that support rather than damage relationships

### Barriers to Emotional Resilience
- Experiential avoidance (ACT) — avoiding feelings through distraction, suppression, substances
- Limited emotional vocabulary (alexithymia)
- Histories where emotional expression was punished or dangerous
- Emotional dysregulation (inability to shift states effectively)

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Emotion identification and labeling skill-building
- Acceptance exercises for difficult emotions (ACT)
- Exposure-based work for emotional avoidance
- Emotional regulation strategy building (DBT-informed where appropriate)

---

## Dimension 5: Somatic-Regulative

### Definition
The capacity to maintain physical regulation, body awareness, and somatic equilibrium — especially during stress and adversity.

### Core Constructs
- **Interoception**: Awareness of internal physical sensations
- **Somatic regulation**: The capacity to return to physiological equilibrium
- **Physical self-care**: Behaviors that sustain physical health and regulation
- **Body-based resilience practices**: Using the body as a resource for resilience

### Behavioral Indicators
- Notices physical signs of stress before they become overwhelming
- Uses somatic strategies (breathing, movement, grounding) effectively
- Maintains basic physical self-care under stress
- Does not dissociate from or override bodily experience

### Barriers to Somatic Resilience
- Histories of trauma stored in the body
- Dissociation from physical experience as a protective adaptation
- Lack of somatic awareness or vocabulary
- Environments where physical self-care is not possible or prioritized

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Body scan and somatic awareness practice-building
- Breathing and nervous system regulation training
- Present-moment awareness through the body (ACT)
- Physical self-care behavioral chains

---

## Dimension 6: Cognitive-Narrative

### Definition
The capacity to construct adaptive meanings, maintain cognitive flexibility, and tell empowering stories about one's experience — especially through and after adversity.

### Core Constructs
- **Cognitive flexibility**: The ability to shift perspectives and consider alternatives
- **Adaptive attribution**: Explaining events in accurate, non-catastrophizing ways
- **Narrative identity**: The story one tells about who they are and why
- **Meaning-making**: Finding or constructing meaning from difficult experience

### Behavioral Indicators
- Can consider multiple perspectives on a situation
- Does not catastrophize or overgeneralize from adversity
- Tells a story of their adversity that includes growth, learning, or strength
- Demonstrates cognitive flexibility under pressure

### Barriers to Cognitive Resilience
- Cognitive fusion with rigid, catastrophizing narratives (ACT)
- Attribution patterns that are global, stable, and internal for negative events
- Absence of a coherent, growth-oriented personal narrative
- Rumination maintained by avoidance of processing

### ABA/ACT Intervention Focus
- Cognitive defusion techniques (ACT)
- Narrative reframing and story work
- Cognitive flexibility training
- Perspective-taking exercises

## Dimensional Interactions

The six dimensions are interdependent. Some common interaction patterns:

| Interaction | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Agentic + Relational** | Agency expressed through and in service of relationship |
| **Emotional + Somatic** | Emotional regulation deeply tied to somatic regulation |
| **Cognitive + Spiritual** | Meaning-making bridges thought and transcendence |
| **Relational + Spiritual** | Community and belonging as spiritual practice |
| **All dimensions** | Each dimension supports and is supported by all others |

---

## Assessment & Measurement

Each dimension is measured by the Resilience Atlas™ 72-question assessment (12 questions per dimension). Dimensional scores provide a profile that is:
- **Normatively referenced** — situating the individual within the broader distribution
- **Ipsative** — showing relative strengths across the individual's own profile
- **Actionable** — directly linked to IATLAS intervention targets
